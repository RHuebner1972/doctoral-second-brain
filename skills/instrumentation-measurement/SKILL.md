# Instrumentation & Measurement: Designing Valid, Reliable Research Tools

What you measure and how you measure it determines what you learn. Whether you're developing a new survey, choosing an existing scale, or designing an interview protocol, this skill guides you through the principles and practices of sound measurement—ensuring your instruments capture what they claim to measure (validity) and do so consistently (reliability).

## Part 1: Instrument Selection vs. Development

### 1.1 When to Use Existing Instruments vs. Develop Your Own

**Use an Existing Instrument If:**
- [ ] An established measure exists for your construct
- [ ] The measure has evidence of reliability and validity
- [ ] The measure has been used in similar populations/contexts
- [ ] You have access to the instrument (permissions/cost)
- [ ] Advantages: Established psychometric properties; comparability to other studies; saves time

**Develop Your Own Instrument If:**
- [ ] No existing instrument captures your specific construct
- [ ] Existing measures don't fit your population/context
- [ ] You need context-specific or culturally-specific items
- [ ] You're studying something novel or emergent
- [ ] You need to combine multiple constructs into one measure
- [ ] Advantages: Perfect fit; can customize; explores new terrain
- [ ] Disadvantages: Requires validation; time-intensive; psychometrics unknown

## Part 2: Developing Instruments

### 2.1 Survey / Scale Development Process

**Phase 1: Conceptualization (Define Your Construct)**

Before writing a single item, define precisely what you're measuring:

**Construct Definition Example:**
```
CONSTRUCT: Teacher Self-Efficacy for Inclusive Teaching

Definition: A teacher's confidence in their ability to effectively 
teach students with diverse learning needs (including students with 
disabilities, ELL students, gifted students) in the regular classroom setting.

Dimensions (what comprises this construct?):
  1. Instructional efficacy (ability to modify curriculum and instruction)
  2. Behavior management efficacy (ability to manage diverse behavior needs)
  3. Collaboration efficacy (ability to work with specialists, families, others)
  4. Resource efficacy (ability to find/use available resources)

Boundary clarity (what is it NOT?):
  ✗ Not general teacher efficacy
  ✗ Not just confidence without skill
  ✗ Not specific to one disability
  ✗ Not administrator or specialist efficacy
```

**Phase 2: Item Generation (Write Candidate Items)**

**Item Writing Principles:**

| Principle | How | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear & Specific** | Avoid ambiguity; one idea per item | Instead of: "I help diverse students"; Better: "I can modify assignments for students with different reading levels" |
| **Appropriate Reading Level** | Match your respondent's education level | Use clear language; avoid jargon unless respondents are experts |
| **Positive Direction** | Phrase most items positively; use reverse-scored items sparingly | Instead of: "I don't struggle with..."; Better: "I can effectively..." |
| **Avoid Doubles** | One construct per item; avoid "and/or" | Instead of: "I feel confident and excited about inclusive teaching"; Better: Two separate items |
| **Avoid Absolutes** | Words like "always," "never" are too extreme; use "usually," "often" | Instead of: "I always differentiate instruction"; Better: "I usually differentiate instruction for different learners" |
| **Concrete Examples** | Specific is better than abstract | Instead of: "I handle diverse student needs"; Better: "I can modify assignments for students reading below grade level" |

**Item Bank Example:**

For each dimension, write 6–10 candidate items:

**Instructional Efficacy:**
1. I can modify lesson plans to meet students' diverse learning needs.
2. I am able to adapt my teaching materials for students with different abilities.
3. I can create learning activities that engage all students in my class.
4. [Write 3–7 more items exploring this dimension]

**Behavior Management Efficacy:**
5. I can manage classroom behavior when students have different behavioral needs.
6. I am able to use positive behavior strategies with students who are disruptive.
[Write 4–9 more items]

[Continue for each dimension]

**Total item bank: 30–50 candidate items**

**Phase 3: Expert Review (Content Validity)**

Have experts (3–5) evaluate items:

- Does each item measure the construct? (Rate on 4-point scale)
- Is the language clear? (Identify confusing items)
- Are items comprehensive? (Any important aspects missing?)
- Are there redundancies? (Items that are too similar?)

**Content Validity Index (CVI):**
Calculate proportion of experts rating item as "essential":
- CVI ≥ 0.78 for 5+ experts = item kept
- CVI < 0.78 = item revised or deleted

**Phase 4: Pilot Testing (Cognitive Interviews)**

Test items with 5–10 people from your target population:

- Read items aloud
- Ask: "What does this item mean to you?"
- Listen for: confusion, unintended meanings, ambiguity
- Revise items based on feedback

**Revised item pool: 25–35 items** (removed redundant/problematic items)

**Phase 5: Psychometric Testing (Reliability & Validity)**

Administer to larger sample (300+ recommended):

**Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA):**
- Tests whether items cluster into expected dimensions
- Removes items that don't load on factors
- Output: 4–5 items per dimension (best-performing items)

**Reliability Testing:**
- Cronbach's alpha per dimension (≥ 0.70 acceptable; ≥ 0.80 good)
- Test-retest reliability (administer twice, 2–4 weeks apart; correlation ≥ 0.70)

**Validity Testing (Sample Evidence):**
- Construct validity: Correlate with theoretically-related measures (should be moderate-to-strong)
- Discriminant validity: Should NOT correlate strongly with unrelated measures
- Criterion validity: If you have outcome data (e.g., teacher practice quality), does the scale predict it?

**Final Scale: 15–20 items** (3–4 per dimension)

### 2.2 Interview Protocol Development

**Phase 1: Determine Interview Type**

| Type | Structure | Best For | Example RQ |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unstructured** | No predetermined questions; conversational | Exploratory; rapport-building | "Tell me about your experience..." |
| **Semi-Structured** | Guide questions; flexible; follow-up probes | Most qualitative research | Guide with 6–10 questions; flexibility for new themes |
| **Structured** | Same questions, same order, little flexibility | Consistency; large-n qualitative samples | Standardized interview; comparable across respondents |

**Phase 2: Develop Question Guide**

**Structure (Semi-Structured Example):**

```
INTERVIEW GUIDE: Teacher Leadership in Inclusive Schools

[5 min] OPENING
- Thank you for participating
- Explain purpose: I'm interested in understanding how teachers develop 
  and enact leadership in schools focused on inclusive education
- Confidentiality: Your responses are confidential; I'll use pseudonyms
- Duration: About 45 minutes
- Recording: I'm recording so I can focus on our conversation

[5 min] RAPPORT & CONTEXT
1. Tell me a bit about your background—how long have you been teaching, 
   and what grade/subject?
2. How would you describe your school's approach to inclusive education?
   
[15 min] EXPERIENCES WITH LEADERSHIP
3. When I say "leadership," what comes to mind for you?
   [Probe: Beyond principal; informal/formal; example of leading]
4. Can you describe a time when you took on a leadership role—formal or informal?
   [Probe: What was the situation? What did you do? What was the outcome?]
5. Tell me about a time when you felt like a leader in your school.
   [Probe: What made it feel like leadership? How did others respond?]

[15 min] PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT IMPACT
6. What professional development related to inclusive teaching have you experienced?
   [Probe: How long? What was covered? Who facilitated?]
7. How (if at all) has this PD changed how you think about teaching?
   [Probe: Specific changes? Shifts in approach? New strategies?]
8. How (if at all) has this PD changed your leadership in your school?
   [Probe: Taken on new roles? Advocated for changes? Mentored colleagues?]

[5 min] BARRIERS & SUPPORTS
9. What supports your leadership around inclusive practice?
   [Probe: People, structures, beliefs, resources]
10. What barriers do you face?
    [Probe: Systemic barriers? Personal? Colleague resistance?]

[3 min] CLOSING
- Is there anything else you'd like me to understand about your experience?
- Thank you for your time and insights.

ESTIMATED TIME: 45–50 minutes
```

**Question-Writing Principles:**

| Principle | How | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Open-Ended** | Allow full responses; no yes/no questions | Instead of: "Did you feel like a leader?"; Better: "Tell me about a time you felt like a leader" |
| **Neutral Language** | Don't lead responses; avoid suggesting answers | Instead of: "What positive changes did you make?"; Better: "What changes, if any, did you make?" |
| **Clear & Specific** | Define terms if needed; be concrete | Instead of: "How has your practice changed?"; Better: "How have you modified your lessons for students with different learning needs?" |
| **One Topic Per Question** | Don't ask two questions at once | Instead of: "What supports and barriers do you face?"; Better: Ask separately |
| **Avoid Jargon** | Use respondent's language, not academic terms | Instead of: "How has your pedagogical practice shifted?"; Better: "How have you changed how you teach?" |

**Probes (Follow-Up Questions):**
- Used when response is vague: "Can you give me an example?"
- Clarification: "What do you mean by [term]?"
- Elaboration: "Tell me more about that"
- Detail: "How did you approach that?"

### 2.3 Observation Protocol / Rubric Development

**For Structured Observation (e.g., classroom observations):**

**Define Each Dimension Clearly:**

```
DIMENSION: Differentiation of Instruction

Definition: The teacher modifies content, process, and/or product to meet 
diverse student learning needs.

Scale:
1 = Not Observed
  - Teacher delivers whole-group instruction; no modifications observed
  - All students complete same task same way
  
2 = Emerging
  - Teacher provides some options (choice board; tiered assignments)
  - But modifications appear ad-hoc, not systematic
  - Limited evidence of addressing diverse learner needs
  
3 = Proficient
  - Teacher uses multiple strategies to differentiate (centers, flexible 
    grouping, tiered tasks, modified materials)
  - Modifications are intentional and aligned to student needs
  - Most students engaged appropriately
  
4 = Advanced
  - Teacher seamlessly differentiates; multiple strategies evident
  - Students appear autonomous in their differentiated activities
  - High engagement across diverse learners
```

**Add Behavioral Anchors (Specific Observable Examples):**
- 1: "All students reading from same textbook page"
- 2: "Two students working on different worksheet; others on textbook"
- 3: "Three different station activities; students appear purposefully assigned"
- 4: "Students choose or are assigned to stations; instructions clear; all engaged; teacher circulates providing differentiated support"

## Part 3: Selecting & Validating Existing Instruments

### 3.1 Finding Existing Instruments

**Where to Look:**

1. **Published Databases:**
   - PubMed (health/medical instruments)
   - PsycINFO (psychology/social science)
   - ERIC (education)
   - Search: "[Construct name] scale OR measure OR instrument"

2. **Open-Access Repositories:**
   - Zenodo (zenodo.org)
   - Open Science Framework (osf.io)
   - ResearchGate (scholars share instruments)

3. **Textbooks & Handbooks:**
   - "Handbook of Scales for Research in Education"
   - Discipline-specific handbooks (cite recent publication)

4. **Author Contact:**
   - Find published studies using the instrument
   - Email corresponding author; ask for instrument + permission

### 3.2 Evaluation Criteria for Existing Instruments

**For Each Potential Instrument, Evaluate:**

| Criterion | What to Look For | Acceptable Standards |
|---|---|---|
| **Reliability** | Cronbach's alpha (internal consistency) | ≥ 0.70 (acceptable); ≥ 0.80 (good) |
| **Validity Evidence** | Construct validity (correlates with theory-related measures) | Documented in published papers; moderate-to-strong correlations |
| **Population Fit** | Has been tested with populations similar to yours | Similar age, education, culture, context |
| **Publication** | Published in peer-reviewed journal | Evidence it's been vetted |
| **Recency** | Recent use/validation | Within last 10–15 years (ensures current relevance) |
| **Language** | Available in language(s) you'll use | Translation validated if not original language |
| **Permissions** | Available for research use | Free OR affordable licensing |
| **Comprehensiveness** | Captures full construct of interest | Multiple items per dimension; not overly brief |

**Instrument Evaluation Template:**

```
INSTRUMENT: [Name]
Author(s): [Citation]

PSYCHOMETRIC PROPERTIES:
  Reliability (Cronbach's α): __________ (acceptable? Y/N)
  Sample size (validity study): __________ (large enough? Y/N)
  Populations tested: __________
  Similar to my population? (Y/N) Why/Why not?

FIT FOR MY STUDY:
  Measures my construct? (Y/N) How well?
  Language appropriate? (Y/N)
  Length reasonable? (Y/N)
  Permissions/Cost: __________

CONCERNS / QUESTIONS:
  [Any reservations?]

RECOMMENDATION:
  ☐ Adopt as-is
  ☐ Adopt with modifications: __________
  ☐ Use as foundation; develop own version
  ☐ Reject; continue searching for alternative
```

### 3.3 If You Modify an Existing Instrument

**If you adapt an existing instrument, you must:**
- [ ] Document every change (what items changed? why?)
- [ ] Justify changes (how do they still measure construct?)
- [ ] Retest reliability & validity with your population
- [ ] Report original source + your modifications in methods
- [ ] Cite original authors

## Part 4: Validity & Reliability Defined

### 4.1 Types of Validity

**Construct Validity** ("Does it measure what it claims?")
- Items accurately measure the construct
- Evidence: Factor analysis shows items load on expected dimensions
- Question: Do the items actually represent this construct?

**Content Validity** ("Are all aspects of the construct covered?")
- Items comprehensively sample the domain
- Evidence: Expert judges agree items represent construct
- Question: Are there important aspects I'm missing?

**Criterion Validity** ("Does it predict/relate to real-world outcomes?")
- Scores correlate with external criteria
- Example: Self-efficacy scale predicts actual teaching quality
- Evidence: Correlation with criterion measure (r ≥ 0.50 good)

**Discriminant Validity** ("Does it NOT measure unrelated constructs?")
- Scores do NOT strongly correlate with unrelated measures
- Example: Self-efficacy should NOT be highly correlated with general self-esteem
- Evidence: Low correlation with unrelated measure (r < 0.30)

### 4.2 Types of Reliability

**Internal Consistency** ("Do items within a scale correlate with each other?")
- Indicates items are measuring the same construct
- Measured by: Cronbach's alpha
- Acceptable: ≥ 0.70; Good: ≥ 0.80; Excellent: ≥ 0.90
- Interpretation: Alpha = 0.80 means items are consistently related

**Test-Retest Reliability** ("Does the measure give consistent results over time?")
- Same respondent, same measure, two different times
- Gap: 2–4 weeks (long enough to avoid memory, short enough construct shouldn't change)
- Measured by: Correlation between two administrations
- Acceptable: r ≥ 0.70; Good: r ≥ 0.80

**Inter-Rater Reliability** ("Do different raters score the same way?")
- Used for observational data, interviews, open-ended responses
- Two or more coders code the same data independently
- Measured by: Cohen's kappa or % agreement
- Acceptable: Cohen's k ≥ 0.60; Good: k ≥ 0.75

## Part 5: Survey Administration & Protocols

### 5.1 Response Scales (Rating Options)

**Likert Scale (Most Common):**
- 4-point: Strongly Disagree, Disagree, Agree, Strongly Agree
- 5-point: Strongly Disagree, Disagree, Neutral, Agree, Strongly Agree
- Recommendation: 4-point (forces choice; avoids fence-sitting)

**Semantic Differential:**
- Bipolar adjectives: Not at all confident ____.____.____. Very confident
- 5–7 points between extremes
- Good for attitudes, feelings

**Visual Analog Scale:**
- Respondent marks point on 10-cm line
- Scored as distance from one end
- Good for pain, moods, intensity

**Ranking:**
- Order items from most to least
- Good for preferences; harder to analyze statistically

### 5.2 Item Response Options

**Rule of Thumb:**
- 4–5 response options optimal (discriminates well; not overwhelming)
- Odd number (allows middle option) OR even number (forces direction)
- 4-point better for lower-literacy groups; 5-point for general population

**Avoid:**
- Too many options (respondents don't discriminate; statistical noise)
- Too few (coarse measurement; loss of information)
- Vague labels ("sometimes" = different things to different people)

## Part 6: Instrument Development Checklist

### Before Administering Your Instrument

- [ ] Instrument designed/selected per principles above
- [ ] Clear, specific items with appropriate language level
- [ ] Instructions written (how to respond, time estimate, confidentiality)
- [ ] Response scale appropriate (4–5 options, clear labels)
- [ ] Items randomized (if possible, to reduce response bias)
- [ ] Demographic questions included (for comparison analysis)
- [ ] Instrument piloted with 5–10 people (confusing items identified/revised)
- [ ] Length acceptable (not so long respondents fatigue)
- [ ] Validity evidence documented (if using existing instrument)
- [ ] Permissions obtained (if using copyrighted instrument)
- [ ] IRB approved (if research involving human subjects)

### During Administration

- [ ] Instructions clear and consistent for all respondents
- [ ] Confidentiality emphasized
- [ ] Time estimate accurate
- [ ] Opportunity for questions
- [ ] Responses properly recorded/stored
- [ ] Data entry checked for accuracy

### Analysis

- [ ] Cronbach's alpha calculated per scale
- [ ] Missing data assessed and handled
- [ ] Reverse-scored items correctly reversed before analysis
- [ ] Scale scores computed per manual/literature
- [ ] Assumptions checked (normality, homogeneity, independence)
- [ ] Planned statistical tests conducted per protocol

## Summary

Sound measurement—whether through developing new instruments or selecting existing ones—is foundational to research quality. Instruments that are unreliable or invalid lead to wrong conclusions. By following these principles, you ensure your measures capture what they claim, do so consistently, and enable you to confidently answer your research questions.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Define** your construct precisely before measuring
2. **Design or select** instruments that fit your population and context
3. **Validate** instruments before using them (or establish validity if using existing)
4. **Pilot test** to catch problems early
5. **Document** everything (changes, validity evidence, reliability statistics)
6. **Be transparent** about instrument limitations and psychometric properties

Your measurements are only as good as your instruments. Choose them carefully.
