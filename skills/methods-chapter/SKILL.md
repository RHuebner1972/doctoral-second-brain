# Methods Chapter

## Purpose
Produce a clear, rigorous Methods chapter that explains *what* you did, *how* you did it, *why* you chose this approach, and how you ensured quality and ethical standards. Your Methods chapter should be detailed enough that another researcher could replicate your study.

## Process

### Step 1 — Gather Context

Ask yourself:
1. What is your research design? (Qualitative, quantitative, mixed methods?)
2. If qualitative: phenomenology, grounded theory, case study, narrative, ethnography, or other?
3. If quantitative: experimental, quasi-experimental, correlational, survey, or other?
4. Who are your participants/subjects? How many? Where from?
5. How will you collect data? (Interviews, surveys, observations, tests, documents, etc.)
6. What analysis procedures will you use?
7. What steps will you take to ensure quality/rigor?

### Step 2 — Structure Your Methods Chapter

**Typical Methods Chapter Structure**

1. **Research Design Overview** (opening section)
   - Your overall approach
   - Rationale for this design

2. **Participants/Sample** (or **Research Context** for qualitative)
   - Who/what did you study?
   - Inclusion/exclusion criteria
   - Sampling method
   - Demographic characteristics

3. **Data Collection**
   - Instruments or procedures
   - Step-by-step process
   - Timeline
   - Setting

4. **Data Analysis**
   - Procedures and tools
   - Step-by-step analytic process
   - Quality assurance measures

5. **Researcher Positionality** (especially qualitative)
   - Your background, biases, role

6. **Validity, Reliability, Trustworthiness**
   - Steps taken to ensure quality

7. **Ethical Considerations**
   - IRB approval
   - Informed consent
   - Confidentiality procedures

### Step 3 — Write Each Section

#### **Section 1: Research Design Overview**

**What to Include**
- State your research design explicitly and early
  - "This is a qualitative phenomenological study..."
  - "This is a quasi-experimental design with pre-test and post-test..."
- Brief justification: Why is this design appropriate for your RQs?
- Connection to your theoretical framework (if applicable)

**Example Opening**
```
This study employed a mixed-methods design with a qualitative-dominant 
perspective (QUAL+quant). This approach was selected because the research 
questions required both in-depth exploration of participants' experiences 
(qualitative) and assessment of patterns across a larger sample (quantitative). 
The qualitative data provided rich context; quantitative data enabled 
generalizability testing.
```

#### **Section 2: Participants/Sample**

**For Qualitative Studies**

- **Sample Size**: How many participants? Be explicit.
  - "Ten public school teachers participated..." (or "n = 10")
  
- **Selection Method**: How did you recruit/select?
  - Purposive sampling? Snowball? Convenience?
  - Justify your sampling strategy: "Purposive sampling was used to ensure 
    participants had direct experience with [phenomenon], which was essential 
    for understanding [RQ]."

- **Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria**
  ```
  Inclusion criteria:
  - Currently employed as a high school math teacher (minimum 3 years experience)
  - Taught in a district with [specific characteristic]
  - Willing to participate in 2 interviews
  
  Exclusion criteria:
  - Teachers without [requirement]
  - Unable to commit to full study timeline
  ```

- **Recruitment & Incentives**
  - How did you find participants? (flyers, email, contacts, etc.)
  - Did you offer compensation? How much?

- **Demographics** (present in table or narrative)
  ```
  Participants ranged in age from 26–58 years (M = 41.2). Nine identified 
  as female, one as male. Teaching experience ranged from 3–22 years (M = 11.4). 
  All taught in mid-size urban districts.
  ```

**For Quantitative Studies**

- **Sample Size & Power Analysis**
  - How many participants did you need? Why?
  - If you conducted a power analysis, describe it:
    - "A priori power analysis (α = .05, 1–β = .80) indicated a minimum 
      sample size of N = 128 participants per group."
  
- **Recruitment Strategy**
  - Where did you recruit? How did you find them?
  - Response rate (if applicable)

- **Demographics**
  - Present in table; describe representativeness
  - Any comparison of respondents vs. non-respondents?

---

#### **Section 3: Data Collection**

**Qualitative Data Collection**

- **Interview Method** (if applicable)
  ```
  Semi-structured interviews lasting 45–60 minutes were conducted in-person 
  (n = 8) or via Zoom (n = 2). A protocol with 12 open-ended questions was 
  used (Appendix A). All interviews were audio-recorded with participant 
  consent and professionally transcribed. Interviews were conducted between 
  [date range].
  ```

- **Observation** (if applicable)
  ```
  Non-participant observations of 3 classroom lessons per teacher (n = 10 
  teachers × 3 observations = 30 total observations) were conducted. Each 
  observation lasted approximately 50 minutes. Observational notes were 
  recorded using a protocol with two sections: (1) descriptive (what occurred) 
  and (2) reflective (researcher impressions and questions).
  ```

- **Document Collection** (if applicable)
  ```
  Curriculum documents (n = 15), student work samples (n = 45), and lesson 
  plans (n = 10) were collected. Documents were selected purposively to 
  represent [criteria].
  ```

- **Instruments**: Provide details
  - Cite the instrument if published
  - Include a sample item or the full instrument in appendix
  - Describe any modifications you made

**Quantitative Data Collection**

- **Instruments**
  ```
  The [Instrument Name] was used to measure [construct]. This 25-item 
  Likert-scale survey demonstrated good reliability in the literature 
  (α = .87; [citation]). For this sample, Cronbach's α = .85.
  ```

- **Procedures**
  ```
  Participants completed the survey online via Qualtrics. The survey took 
  approximately 15 minutes. Data were collected over a 4-week period 
  (March–April 2024).
  ```

- **Timing & Setting**
  - When? How long? Where?

---

#### **Section 4: Data Analysis**

**Qualitative Analysis**

- **Coding Approach**
  ```
  Data were analyzed using thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's 
  (2006) six-step procedure. Initial open coding was conducted line-by-line 
  on 2 transcripts to develop a preliminary codebook (Appendix B). This 
  codebook was then applied to all 10 interviews. Codes were organized into 
  candidate themes, reviewed against the data, and refined until thematic 
  saturation was reached.
  ```

- **Software**
  - Did you use NVivo, ATLAS.ti, MAXQDA, or manual coding?
  - If manual: how did you organize codes?

- **Analytic Decisions**
  - Why this approach? (e.g., "Phenomenological analysis was chosen to honor 
    participants' lived experiences and meaning-making.")
  - Any deviation from standard procedures? (e.g., "Constant comparison was 
    adapted slightly to accommodate [reason].")

**Quantitative Analysis**

- **Statistical Tests**
  ```
  Descriptive statistics (means, standard deviations) were calculated for 
  all variables. An independent samples t-test was conducted to compare 
  [outcome] between [groups]. Assumptions (normality, equal variances) 
  were tested using [procedures]. Multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) 
  was used to examine differences on [multiple outcomes] simultaneously.
  ```

- **Statistical Software**
  - SPSS, R, Stata, SAS?
  - Version number?

- **Significance Level & Effect Sizes**
  ```
  Statistical significance was set at α = .05. Effect sizes (Cohen's d, 
  eta-squared) were reported alongside p-values to assess practical significance.
  ```

#### **Section 5: Researcher Positionality** (Qualitative primarily)

Acknowledge your role and potential biases:
```
I am a former high school math teacher with 8 years of experience and 
personal investment in mathematics education reform. This background 
informed my interest in this study and likely shaped my interpretation 
of participants' experiences. To mitigate bias, I used reflexive journaling 
throughout data collection and analysis, consulted with my advisor on 
interpretations, and employed member checking (see Trustworthiness section).
```

---

#### **Section 6: Validity, Reliability, Trustworthiness**

**For Qualitative Studies (using trustworthiness criteria)**

- **Credibility** (equivalent to internal validity)
  ```
  Member checking was conducted; three participants reviewed emerging themes 
  and confirmed interpretations. Prolonged engagement with data (80+ hours 
  across 4 months) ensured deep understanding. Triangulation across interview, 
  observation, and document data strengthened credibility.
  ```

- **Transferability** (equivalent to external validity)
  ```
  Detailed descriptions of the research context, participants, and procedures 
  enable readers to assess transferability. Study limitations (regional focus, 
  specific participant population) are acknowledged in the Discussion.
  ```

- **Dependability** (equivalent to reliability)
  ```
  An audit trail documenting all analytical decisions, code development, and 
  theme refinement was maintained throughout analysis. A codebook was kept 
  and shared with the research team for transparency.
  ```

- **Confirmability** (equivalent to objectivity)
  ```
  Bracketing and reflexivity strategies were used to minimize researcher bias. 
  Raw data (interview transcripts, observation notes) were reviewed by the 
  research team to confirm interpretations.
  ```

**For Quantitative Studies (using validity criteria)**

- **Internal Validity**
  ```
  The quasi-experimental design with pre-test/post-test control reduced threats 
  to internal validity. Matching procedures ensured groups were comparable at 
  baseline. Statistical controls for [variable] were used to account for 
  [potential confound].
  ```

- **External Validity**
  ```
  The random sampling procedure enhanced the representativeness of the sample. 
  However, findings may not generalize to [populations/settings outside the 
  sample], which limits external validity.
  ```

- **Construct Validity**
  ```
  Instruments were selected based on established reliability and validity in 
  the literature. [Instrument] was validated with populations similar to this 
  sample. Convergent validity between [measure A] and [measure B] was confirmed 
  (r = .78).
  ```

- **Statistical Conclusion Validity**
  ```
  Assumptions for statistical tests were verified. Power analysis confirmed 
  adequate sample size. Effect sizes are reported alongside p-values.
  ```

---

#### **Section 7: Ethical Considerations**

```
This study received Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval (#12345) from 
[University Name] on [date]. Informed consent was obtained from all participants 
prior to data collection. Participants were informed of their right to withdraw 
without penalty.

Confidentiality was maintained through [procedures]. All identifying information 
was removed and replaced with participant ID codes (P1–P10). Interview recordings 
were stored on an encrypted, password-protected drive. Transcripts will be 
destroyed [X years] after study completion per IRB requirements. No participant 
names appear in publications or presentations.

Risks to participants were minimal; the main risk was potential emotional 
discomfort during interviews about [sensitive topic]. To mitigate this, 
participants were offered breaks, given the option to skip questions, and 
provided with [resource/referral].
```

### Step 4 — Align Methods With Research Questions

Create a mapping to ensure each RQ is addressed:

| Research Question | How Addressed in Methods |
|------------------|------------------------|
| What are teachers' experiences with [X]? | Semi-structured interviews (n=10); thematic analysis |
| Does [intervention] improve [outcome]? | Quasi-experimental design; independent samples t-test |
| How do [variables] interact? | MANOVA analyzing [outcomes] by [factors] |

Every RQ should be answered; if a section of your methods doesn't connect to an RQ, consider whether it's necessary.

---

### Step 5 — Common Methods Chapter Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Too much detail about irrelevant procedures**
- Include what's necessary; move highly technical detail to appendices

❌ **Passive voice that obscures your role**
- "Data were collected" vs. "I collected data through..."
- Mix active and passive appropriately

❌ **Vague descriptions that couldn't be replicated**
- Instead of "interviews were conducted," say "semi-structured interviews 
  averaging 52 minutes, guided by a 12-item protocol, were conducted with 
  each participant. Two interviews were in-person; eight were via Zoom."

❌ **Insufficient justification for methodological choices**
- Not just *what* you did, but *why* this approach was appropriate

❌ **Misalignment between methods and RQs**
- If RQ asks about lived experience, but you use a closed-ended survey, 
  that's misaligned

❌ **Overlooking ethical considerations**
- IRB approval, informed consent, confidentiality procedures must be explicit

## Methods Chapter Checklist

- [ ] Clearly state your research design and justify it
- [ ] Describe your participants/sample comprehensively (size, selection, demographics)
- [ ] Detail your data collection procedures (instruments, timing, setting)
- [ ] Explain your data analysis approach step-by-step
- [ ] Address researcher positionality (especially if qualitative)
- [ ] Discuss measures taken to ensure validity/reliability/trustworthiness
- [ ] Explicitly address ethical considerations (IRB, consent, confidentiality)
- [ ] Align methods with research questions (each RQ addressed)
- [ ] Provide sufficient detail for replication
- [ ] Organize using clear headings and subheadings
- [ ] Use tables/figures for complex information (demographics, coding schemes)
- [ ] Check that methods align with your proposal (note any changes & justify)

---

## Methods Chapter Strengths Indicators

✓ Design is clearly justified; another researcher could replicate the study  
✓ Participant/sample description is comprehensive and transparent  
✓ Data collection & analysis procedures are described in sufficient detail  
✓ Quality/rigor measures are appropriate to the methodology  
✓ Ethical considerations are thoroughly addressed  
✓ Methods align with research questions  
✓ Any deviations from original proposal are acknowledged & justified
