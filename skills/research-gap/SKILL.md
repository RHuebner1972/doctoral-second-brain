# Research Gap Analysis

## Purpose
Help the learner identify a defensible, meaningful gap in the existing literature — one that justifies a new study.

## Process

### Step 1 — Gather Context
Ask the learner:
1. What is your general topic or field of interest?
2. What have you already read? (journals, authors, key studies)
3. What degree program and methodology orientation are you in (qualitative, quantitative, mixed)?
4. Is there a population, context, or phenomenon you're drawn to?

### Step 2 — Analyze the Four Gap Types

Work through each lens systematically:

**Population Gap**
Who has NOT been studied? Underrepresented groups, minority populations, non-Western contexts, specific industries, career stages, or organizational types not represented in existing research.

**Methodological Gap**
How has this topic been studied — and what methods are absent? If most studies are quantitative surveys, a qualitative phenomenological lens may reveal what numbers can't capture. If most are small case studies, a large-n comparative study may be needed.

**Contextual/Situational Gap**
Where has this NOT been studied? Specific geographic regions, organizational settings, sectors, time periods, or environmental conditions that haven't been examined.

**Temporal Gap**
When was the most recent foundational research done? Has the world changed enough (technology, policy, demographics) that findings from 10–15 years ago may no longer hold?

**Conceptual/Theoretical Gap**
What theoretical lens or construct has not been applied to this phenomenon? Are there emerging frameworks in adjacent fields that haven't crossed into this literature yet?

### Step 3 — Draft the Gap Statement

Use this template:
> "Although [existing research has addressed X], little is known about [specific population/context/method/phenomenon]. No studies have examined [the gap] in [setting/group], leaving [practical/theoretical consequence] unaddressed."

### Step 4 — Test the Gap
Ask:
- Is this gap researchable? Can you collect data on it?
- Is this gap meaningful? Would closing it matter to theory, practice, or policy?
- Is this gap yours? Do you have access to this population or context?

## Output Format
Return a structured gap analysis with:
1. **Gap type(s) identified** (label each)
2. **Evidence of the gap** (what you found or didn't find in the literature)
3. **Draft gap statement** (2–3 sentences, scholarly register)
4. **Researchability check** (yes/partially/no — and why)
5. **Next step** (problem statement, or more literature searching)
