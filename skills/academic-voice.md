# Academic Voice Editor

## Overview

Academic writing at the doctoral level has a distinct register: precise, formal, evidence-grounded, and carefully hedged. It is not the same as being stuffy or passive — the best dissertation writing is clear, direct, and specific. This skill edits your drafts for scholarly voice, flags common problems, and provides rewritten alternatives.

## The Six Dimensions of Academic Voice

### 1. Precision — Say Exactly What You Mean

**Vague:** "Many researchers have looked at leadership."
**Precise:** "Scholars have examined the relationship between servant leadership and employee retention across multiple organizational sectors (Liden et al., 2008; van Dierendonck, 2011)."

Rules:
- Replace vague quantifiers (*many, some, a lot*) with specific claims or citations
- Name the specific constructs, populations, and relationships
- Avoid "things," "aspects," "factors," "issues" — be specific

### 2. Formality — Match the Register

**Informal:** "Leaders need to get their act together when it comes to retention."
**Formal:** "Leadership behaviors are increasingly recognized as a primary determinant of employee retention outcomes (CUPA-HR, 2022)."

Words to avoid:
| Informal | Formal Alternative |
|---|---|
| a lot of | a substantial body of / numerous |
| get | obtain / achieve / yield |
| show | demonstrate / indicate / reveal |
| talk about | address / examine / discuss |
| look at | investigate / analyze / examine |
| find out | determine / identify / ascertain |
| use | employ / utilize (but don't over-utilize "utilize") |
| ok / fine | acceptable / appropriate / satisfactory |

### 3. Hedging — Calibrate Your Claims

Academic writing does not overclaim. Use hedging language to signal appropriate epistemic humility — especially in interpretations and conclusions.

**Overclaiming:** "This study proves that servant leadership causes retention."
**Hedged:** "Findings suggest that servant leadership behaviors may be associated with increased faculty retention intention."

Hedging vocabulary:
- *may / might / could*
- *suggests / indicates / implies*
- *appears to / seems to*
- *is consistent with*
- *warrants further investigation*
- *it is possible that*

**Do NOT hedge when stating established facts:**
❌ "It may be the case that the earth orbits the sun."
✓ "The study found a statistically significant positive correlation (r = .43, p < .001)."

### 4. Third Person — Remove the "I" (Usually)

Most dissertation programs require third-person throughout (except reflexivity sections in qualitative work).

**First person:** "I believe servant leadership is important."
**Third person:** "Servant leadership is widely recognized as a determinant of positive organizational outcomes."

*Exception: Qualitative researchers should discuss their positionality and reflexivity in first person in Chapter 3's role of the researcher section — check your program's guidelines.*

### 5. Evidence Grounding — Don't Assert, Cite

**Assertion:** "Leadership is one of the most studied topics in organizational research."
**Grounded:** "Leadership has been one of the most frequently studied topics in organizational scholarship, yielding thousands of empirical studies over five decades (Bass & Riggio, 2006)."

Rule: Every factual claim must be supported by a citation. Your interpretation of evidence is acceptable without a citation — but make clear it is your interpretation.

### 6. Concision — Cut What Doesn't Work

Doctoral writing often becomes bloated with filler. Common culprits:

| Wordy | Concise |
|---|---|
| In order to | To |
| Due to the fact that | Because |
| It is important to note that | [Delete — just say the thing] |
| At this point in time | Currently / now |
| In the field of education | In education |
| The purpose of this section is to | [Just start the section] |
| It can be seen that | [Delete] |
| There are many researchers who | Many researchers |

## Common Patterns and Fixes

### Problem: Passive Overuse
APA encourages active voice. Passive voice is acceptable when the agent is unknown or irrelevant, but avoid it when you can be specific.

**Passive:** "The survey was administered to participants."
**Active:** "Participants completed an online survey."

**Passive:** "It was found that…"
**Active:** "Results indicated that…" or "Analysis revealed that…"

### Problem: Weak Transitions
**Weak:** "Also, another study looked at this."
**Strong:** "Building on this foundation, [Author] extended the investigation to [population/context], finding that [result]."

### Problem: Over-quoting
Use direct quotes sparingly in dissertations — they should be reserved for definitions, key theoretical statements, or passages where precise wording matters. Otherwise, paraphrase.

### Problem: Undefined Constructs
Every key term in your dissertation must be operationally defined the first time it appears, with a citation.

**Undefined:** "This study examines servant leadership among faculty."
**Defined:** "This study examines servant leadership, defined as 'a set of leader behaviors that prioritize follower needs and development' (Liden et al., 2008, p. 162), among community college faculty."

## Before / After Examples

**Before:**
> "Leadership is really important in schools. A lot of research has shown that when leaders are good, teachers stay. This study will look at how servant leadership affects teacher retention."

**After:**
> "Effective leadership is widely recognized as a determinant of teacher retention in K–12 and post-secondary settings (Ingersoll & Strong, 2011; Leithwood et al., 2019). Servant leadership, characterized by leaders who prioritize follower needs and professional development (Greenleaf, 1977; Liden et al., 2008), represents a promising yet understudied leadership approach in educational contexts. This study examines the relationship between perceived servant leadership behaviors and faculty retention intention in community college settings."

## Editing Checklist

- [ ] No contractions (it's → it is; don't → do not)
- [ ] No first person (unless in qualitative reflexivity section)
- [ ] No vague quantifiers without citations
- [ ] All key constructs defined and cited on first use
- [ ] Claims are hedged appropriately (not under-, not over-)
- [ ] Transitions connect ideas explicitly
- [ ] No filler phrases (it is important to note, at this point in time, etc.)
- [ ] Direct quotes are used sparingly; paraphrase is preferred
- [ ] Consistent tense throughout each section
