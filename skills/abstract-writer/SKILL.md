# Abstract Writer

## Purpose
Draft a precise, complete abstract for a dissertation, journal submission, or conference — capturing the study's purpose, methodology, findings, and significance within tight word limits.

## Types of Abstracts

### Dissertation Abstract (Unstructured)
- **Length:** 150–350 words (varies by institution; Walden/GCU often 200–300)
- **Format:** Single paragraph, no headings
- **Tense:** Past tense for completed studies; future tense for proposals

### Journal Article Abstract — Structured
- **Length:** 150–300 words
- **Format:** Labeled sections (Background, Objective, Methods, Results, Conclusions)
- **Tense:** Past tense throughout

### Journal Article Abstract — Unstructured
- **Length:** 150–250 words
- **Format:** Single dense paragraph
- **Tense:** Past tense

### Conference Abstract
- **Length:** 100–500 words (highly variable; check the CFP)
- **Format:** May be structured or unstructured depending on conference
- **Goal:** Persuade reviewers — include significance and contribution prominently

## The Six Elements of a Strong Abstract

Every abstract should contain:
1. **Purpose** — What was the study about? What problem did it address?
2. **Gap** — What was missing from the literature? (1 sentence)
3. **Methodology** — What design, approach, and data collection were used?
4. **Sample** — Who participated? How many? What context?
5. **Findings** — What did the study find? (most important element — be specific)
6. **Significance** — What do these findings mean for practice, theory, or policy?

For proposals (not yet completed), replace Findings with:
5. **Anticipated Contributions** — What the study is expected to reveal

## Process

### Step 1 — Gather Inputs
Ask the learner:
1. What is the type and target (dissertation, journal name, conference)?
2. What is the word limit?
3. What was the purpose and research questions?
4. What methodology and design was used?
5. What were the key findings?
6. What are the implications?

### Step 2 — Draft the Abstract

**Dissertation abstract template:**
> The problem of [problem] is significant because [consequence] ([citation]). Although [existing research], [gap] remains unaddressed. The purpose of this [design] study was to [purpose statement]. The [theoretical framework] guided this study. [N] participants were recruited from [context] using [sampling strategy]. Data were collected through [methods] and analyzed using [analysis approach]. Findings revealed [key finding 1], [key finding 2], and [key finding 3]. These findings suggest that [implication for practice]. Future research should [recommendation]. This study contributes to [field/theory/practice] by [specific contribution].

**Structured journal abstract template:**
> **Background:** [Context and significance]
> **Objective:** [Purpose statement]
> **Methods:** [Design, sample, data collection, analysis]
> **Results:** [Key findings — be specific with themes or statistics]
> **Conclusions:** [Implications and contributions]

### Step 3 — Refine for Length and Precision
- Cut all adjectives that don't carry meaning
- Every sentence must carry one specific piece of information
- Remove hedging from the findings section (you found it — state it)
- Cut phrases like "This study aimed to..." — replace with active statements

## Common Abstract Errors
- Vague findings: "Themes emerged" → Name the themes
- Missing significance: describe what was done but not why it matters
- Over-explaining methodology at the expense of findings
- Exceeding word limit
- Proposal abstracts that don't acknowledge it's a proposal

## Output Format
1. **Draft abstract** (word count noted)
2. **Element check** (does each of the 6 elements appear?)
3. **Revision notes** (what's missing or could be sharper)
4. **Keywords** (5–7 keywords for indexing, if needed)
