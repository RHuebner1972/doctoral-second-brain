# Dissertation Proposal Writing & Defense: Crafting & Defending Your Research Plan

Your dissertation proposal is your research contract with your committee: it says "here's what I'm going to study, here's why, here's how, and here's what I expect to find." This skill guides you through writing a compelling, rigorous proposal and then defending it—answering questions, addressing concerns, and demonstrating that you've thought through the work ahead.

## Part 1: The Dissertation Proposal's Purpose

A proposal serves four critical functions:

1. **Intellectual Accountability** — Shows your committee that your problem, questions, methods, and timeline are well-reasoned
2. **Approval Gate** — Confirms that your study is feasible, ethical, and valuable before you invest months in data collection
3. **Contract** — Establishes expectations: what you said you'd do is what you'll be evaluated on at defense
4. **IRB/Ethics Bridge** — Serves as foundation for IRB protocol; demonstrates ethical thinking

## Part 2: Proposal Structure & Content

### 2.1 Standard Proposal Outline

**Typical length:** 30–50 pages (varies by program; check with your department)

**Standard Structure:**

```
I. INTRODUCTION & PROBLEM STATEMENT (3–5 pages)
   1.1 Hook / Opening Context
   1.2 Problem Statement (why this matters)
   1.3 Significance (contribution to field)
   1.4 Scope & Limitations (what you're NOT studying)
   1.5 Dissertation Overview (roadmap)

II. LITERATURE REVIEW & THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK (10–15 pages)
    2.1 Domain 1 (e.g., Inclusive Education)
    2.2 Domain 2 (e.g., Teacher Leadership)
    2.3 Integration / Intersection (where your study lives)
    2.4 Theoretical Framework (lens for interpretation)
    2.5 Research Gap (synthesis → gap → RQ)

III. RESEARCH QUESTIONS & SIGNIFICANCE (2–3 pages)
     3.1 Central Research Question
     3.2 Sub-questions (if any)
     3.3 Significance (who cares? why now?)

IV. METHODOLOGY (8–12 pages)
    4.1 Research Design (paradigm, approach)
    4.2 Participants / Sample (recruitment, inclusion/exclusion)
    4.3 Data Collection (instruments, procedures, timeline)
    4.4 Data Analysis (coding scheme, statistical tests)
    4.5 Trustworthiness / Validity (credibility measures)

V. TIMELINE & FEASIBILITY (1–2 pages)
   5.1 Milestones & Timeline (month-by-month or semester)
   5.2 Feasibility Statement (resources, access, constraints)

VI. ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS (1–2 pages)
    6.1 IRB Approval Status / Plan
    6.2 Informed Consent Strategy
    6.3 Risk Minimization
    6.4 Confidentiality / Data Security

VII. REFERENCES (follows APA format)

[APPENDICES: Instruments, protocols, timelines, sample materials]
```

### 2.2 Detailed Section Guidance

**I. INTRODUCTION & PROBLEM STATEMENT**

Purpose: Hook the reader and establish why your study matters

**Opening Paragraph (Hook):**
- Start with a compelling fact, observation, or question—not abstract theory
- Example: "Inclusion has been policy for three decades, yet 67% of students with disabilities still spend half their day outside regular classrooms. Why?"
- Make the reader care immediately

**Problem Statement:**
- Concise (1 paragraph): what is the problem, who experiences it, why is it important?
- Grounded in evidence (cite data)
- Specific (not so broad it encompasses everything)

Example problem statement:
```
Despite policy mandates for inclusive education, research shows that 
teachers often feel unprepared to support students with disabilities in 
inclusive settings. Professional development exists but varies widely in 
quality and approach. This study addresses the gap between teachers' 
stated need for inclusive pedagogy training and the limited evidence on 
what training approaches are most effective in changing classroom practice.
```

**Significance:**
- How does your study advance the field?
- Who will benefit? (researchers, practitioners, policymakers, students)
- What problem will solving this address?

**I. Scope & Limitations:**
- What are you NOT studying? (important for clarity)
- Example: "This study focuses on secondary teachers, not elementary; inclusion of students with disabilities, not other equity issues; urban schools only"

---

**II. LITERATURE REVIEW & THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK**

Purpose: Show that you know the field and understand where your study fits

**Section 2.1–2.3 (Domain Reviews):**
- Organize thematically, not chronologically
- Each paragraph: one claim + 2–3 supporting citations
- Synthesize (don't just summarize)
- Show evolution of thinking: "Early studies focused on X; recent research shows Y; we still don't know Z"

Example structure:
```
INCLUSIVE EDUCATION IMPLEMENTATION
  → Policy landscape (IDEA, LRE mandate)
  → Barriers to inclusion (teacher beliefs, resource constraints, training gaps)
  → Effective inclusion practices (collaborative planning, co-teaching, PLC models)

TEACHER LEADERSHIP
  → Definitions and frameworks
  → Leadership development pathways
  → Distributed vs. formal leadership models
  → Teacher leadership in inclusive schools (emerging research)

PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT FOR INCLUSIVE PRACTICE
  → Characteristics of effective PD
  → Evidence on PD impact (and lack thereof)
  → Specific models for inclusive pedagogy (what works? for whom?)
```

**Section 2.4 (Theoretical Framework):**
- Choose one lens that will guide your interpretation
- Examples: Social constructivism, Situated Learning Theory, Communities of Practice, Ecological Systems Theory
- Explain: Why this lens? How does it shape your questions and interpretation?

Example:
```
THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK: Communities of Practice (CoP)

Wenger's (1998) Communities of Practice framework posits that learning 
occurs through participation in communities of practitioners with shared 
interests and mutual engagement. This lens is appropriate for this study 
because: (1) teacher leadership emerges through participation in school 
communities; (2) inclusive practice develops through shared practice and 
discourse; (3) PD is most effective when it builds collaborative communities, 
not isolated individual learning.

From this lens, I interpret teacher leadership as emerging through 
participation in school communities focused on inclusive practice, and I 
examine how PD creates or strengthens these communities.
```

**Section 2.5 (Research Gap & RQ):**
- Synthesis of all prior sections: what do we know?
- Gap: what's missing? what's unanswered?
- Your RQ emerges from the gap

Example:
```
Summary of Literature:
Research shows that:
  ✓ Inclusive education policy exists but implementation is uneven
  ✓ Teachers feel unprepared for inclusive practice
  ✓ Professional development exists but quality varies
  ✓ Teacher leadership matters for school improvement
  
What we don't know:
  ✗ How does PD on inclusive practice develop teacher leadership?
  ✗ What PD characteristics best support leadership development?
  ✗ What is the mechanism by which PD changes practice?
  ✗ How do teachers enact leadership in inclusive settings?

This study addresses these gaps by examining:
  [Lead into your Research Questions...]
```

**III. RESEARCH QUESTIONS & SIGNIFICANCE**

Purpose: State clearly and specifically what you're studying

**Central Research Question (RQ):**
- One overarching question that your study aims to answer
- Broad enough to guide inquiry; specific enough to be answerable
- Open-ended (not yes/no)
- Example: "How do teachers develop leadership capacity through professional development focused on inclusive practice?"

**Sub-Questions (if applicable):**
- 2–4 follow-up questions that break down the central RQ
- Examples:
  1. "What aspects of PD do teachers perceive as supporting leadership development?"
  2. "How do teachers translate PD learning into classroom practice and school advocacy?"
  3. "What barriers impede teachers' enactment of leadership around inclusion?"

**Significance:**
- Why should the field care about answers to these questions?
- What's at stake? (student outcomes, teacher efficacy, policy implementation)
- Who will use these findings? (practitioners, researchers, policymakers)

**IV. METHODOLOGY**

Purpose: Show that you have a sound, feasible plan to answer your RQs

**4.1 Research Design:**
- Paradigm: qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods
- Approach: phenomenology, case study, RCT, survey, etc.
- Justification: Why is this design appropriate for your RQs?

Example:
```
RESEARCH DESIGN: Qualitative Case Study

This study employs a collective case study design (Stake, 2006) to examine 
teacher leadership development in three secondary schools implementing 
inclusive education reform. Case study is appropriate because:
  1. RQs are exploratory ("how do teachers develop...") requiring deep 
     understanding of context
  2. Leadership development is context-dependent; comparing multiple school 
     contexts allows investigation of contextual factors
  3. Qualitative data (interviews, observations) capture teachers' 
     experiences and sense-making
  4. Case study design allows integration of multiple data sources, 
     increasing credibility
```

**4.2 Participants / Sample:**

Describe who you'll study; how you'll recruit them; criteria for inclusion:

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sample Size** | 15–20 teachers (qualitative); specify how determined (saturation, power analysis, etc.) |
| **Selection Criteria** | Inclusion: [e.g., secondary teachers; 3+ years experience; in school implementing inclusion reform]; Exclusion: [e.g., administrators, special ed coordinators] |
| **Recruitment Strategy** | How will you find and recruit participants? (e.g., contact school principals; flyers; purposeful sampling) |
| **Anticipated Challenges** | What might make recruitment difficult? How will you address? |
| **Demographic Diversity** | How will you ensure diverse sample? (e.g., across subject areas, race/ethnicity, gender, school context) |

**4.3 Data Collection:**

Specify what data you'll collect, how, when, and why:

| Data Source | Method | Frequency | Duration | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Interviews** | Semi-structured; recorded and transcribed | 2 per participant (baseline + post-PD) | 45–60 min each | Captures teachers' experiences, sense-making, leadership perceptions |
| **Classroom Observations** | Structured observation (rubric); field notes; video record | 2 per participant (baseline + post-PD) | 50-min class period | Documents practice changes; grounds RQs in actual behavior |
| **Focus Group** | 1 per school; 8–10 teachers | Once, at project end | 90 min | Explores collective sense-making; identifies systemic factors |
| **Documents** | School mission, inclusion policy, PD curriculum, meeting notes | As available | — | Provides context; shows institutional priorities |
| **Researcher Reflexivity Journal** | Ongoing; entries after each interview/observation | After each encounter | 15–30 min | Documents researcher assumptions; addresses bias |

**4.4 Data Analysis:**

Specify how you'll analyze the data:

*For Qualitative:*
```
QUALITATIVE DATA ANALYSIS

Phase 1: Data Preparation (Months 6–7)
  - Transcribe all interviews (or arrange transcription service)
  - Type observation field notes into standard format
  - Anonymize data (replace names with pseudonyms)
  - Import into NVivo for coding

Phase 2: Initial Coding (Months 7–8)
  - Open coding: initial read-through; label meaningful units
  - Both inductive (emerge from data) and deductive (theory-driven) codes
  - Create preliminary codebook
  
Phase 3: Codebook Development (Months 8–9)
  - Organize codes into categories
  - Define each code with exemplars
  - Test codebook by coding 2–3 interviews; refine
  - Use codebook for remaining data

Phase 4: Candidate Theme Development (Months 9–10)
  - Review all coded data
  - Identify patterns across codes
  - Group codes into candidate themes
  - Ensure each theme is supported by multiple instances

Phase 5: Theme Refinement (Months 10–11)
  - Review raw data again; validate themes
  - Check coherence: does each theme make sense? Are boundaries clear?
  - Collapse/merge similar themes; split themes lacking coherence
  - Define each theme; write theme descriptions

Phase 6: Synthesis & Narrative Development (Months 11–12)
  - Write findings narrative organized by themes
  - Integrate quotes; show variation within themes
  - Relate themes to research questions
  - Draw implications
```

*For Quantitative:*
```
QUANTITATIVE DATA ANALYSIS

Phase 1: Data Screening (Week 1–2)
  - Assess normality of distributions (histograms, Q–Q plots)
  - Check for outliers (z-scores > 3.29)
  - Assess missing data patterns (MCAR vs. MAR)
  
Phase 2: Descriptive Statistics (Week 2–3)
  - Means, standard deviations, frequencies
  - Create Table 1 (sample characteristics by group)
  - Report univariate statistics
  
Phase 3: Assumption Checking (Week 3–4)
  - Homogeneity of variance (Levene's test)
  - Independence of observations
  - Linearity (scatter plots)
  
Phase 4: Inferential Tests (Week 4–6)
  - Based on RQs: t-tests, ANOVA, regression, etc.
  - Report test statistics, p-values, effect sizes
  - Conduct follow-up tests as needed (post-hoc comparisons)
  
Phase 5: Sensitivity Analyses (Week 6–7)
  - Test robustness of findings
  - Repeat analyses excluding outliers
  - Test with different statistical approaches
  
Phase 6: Results Summary (Week 7–8)
  - Create tables and figures
  - Write results section
  - Prepare interpretations for discussion
```

**4.5 Trustworthiness / Validity:**

For qualitative, explain how you'll ensure credibility; for quantitative, address validity:

*Qualitative Trustworthiness:*
```
TRUSTWORTHINESS STRATEGIES

Credibility (Are findings believable?):
  ✓ Prolonged engagement: 12-month study allows deep understanding
  ✓ Persistent observation: Multiple interviews/observations per participant
  ✓ Member checking: Share preliminary findings with 5 participants; 
    ask: "Does this match your experience?"
  ✓ Peer debriefing: Meet with [advisor name] monthly; discuss findings, 
    challenge interpretations

Transferability (Can findings apply elsewhere?):
  ✓ Thick description: Detailed descriptions of context, participants, and 
    findings allow readers to assess applicability
  ✓ Multiple contexts: Study 3 schools in different districts; explore 
    contextual variation

Dependability (Is process transparent and auditable?):
  ✓ Audit trail: Document all decisions; maintain processing log
  ✓ Reflexivity: Journal entries address researcher assumptions, biases
  ✓ Code-recode: Spot-check coding consistency (re-code 10% of data 
    two weeks later; compare)

Confirmability (Are findings grounded in data, not researcher bias?):
  ✓ Data audit: Ask [colleague name] to review sample of raw data and 
    coded data; assess alignment
  ✓ Negative case analysis: Seek and discuss data that contradict 
    preliminary findings
```

*Quantitative Validity:*
```
VALIDITY CONSIDERATIONS

Internal Validity (Can we infer causation?):
  - Design: Quasi-experimental with control group minimizes threats
  - Homogeneous sample: Controls for participant differences
  - Measurement: Validated instruments reduce measurement error
  - Analysis: Control for confounding variables via regression

External Validity (Can findings generalize?):
  - Diverse sample across demographics, districts, school types
  - Purposeful sampling captures variation
  - Results may not generalize to [specific exclusion: private schools, 
    elementary, rural]

Construct Validity (Are we measuring what we claim?):
  - Use validated measures (cite validity evidence)
  - Multiple items per construct (reduce single-item bias)
  - Convergent validity: Correlate with theoretically related constructs
  
Statistical Conclusion Validity:
  - Adequate sample size (powered for RQs)
  - Reliable instruments (report alphas)
  - Appropriate statistical tests for data
```

## Part 3: Writing Tips for Proposals

### 3.1 Tone & Register

**Proposal Voice:**
- Academic, professional, confident (not tentative)
- Say "will collect data" not "hope to collect"; "the study examines" not "the study may examine"
- Third person typically (though some programs accept "I will...")
- Precise language (don't say "explore" if you mean "examine"; don't say "look at" in formal writing)

**Balancing Conviction with Humility:**
- Conviction: "This research addresses a critical gap in understanding..."
- Humility: "While this study cannot answer all questions about X, it will illuminate..."

### 3.2 Common Proposal Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Vague RQs** | "How do teachers develop?" | "How do teachers develop leadership capacity through professional development focused on inclusive practice?" |
| **Unsupported claims** | "Inclusion improves outcomes" | "Inclusion has been shown to improve academic and social-emotional outcomes [citation]" |
| **Underdeveloped methodology** | "I'll interview teachers" (no detail) | "I will conduct 2 semi-structured interviews (45–60 min) with 15 purposefully-sampled teachers from 3 schools..." |
| **Disconnected theory & RQ** | Theory is about motivation; RQ is about leadership (no connection) | Show explicitly how theory shapes interpretation of findings |
| **Unfeasible timeline** | Collect data, transcribe, code, analyze, write in 4 months | Realistic: 6 months collection, 3 months coding, 3 months analysis/writing = 12 months |
| **Missing ethical considerations** | No mention of IRB or risk | Spell out: IRB approval status, informed consent process, confidentiality protections, risk minimization |

## Part 4: Preparing for Proposal Defense

### 4.1 Defense Preparation Timeline

**6 Weeks Before:**
- [ ] Distribute draft proposal to committee (ask for initial feedback)
- [ ] Read committee members' recent publications (understand their interests)
- [ ] Create 15–20 minute presentation summary
- [ ] Anticipate 5–10 likely questions (see below)

**3 Weeks Before:**
- [ ] Incorporate committee feedback; revise proposal
- [ ] Practice presentation (out loud, timed)
- [ ] Create visual slides (problem statement, RQs, methodology, timeline, significance)
- [ ] Request clarification on committee members' specific concerns

**2 Weeks Before:**
- [ ] Know your proposal inside-out (be able to cite specific pages/sections)
- [ ] Prepare 1-page executive summary
- [ ] Create one-page timeline visual
- [ ] Identify 2–3 potential limitations (committees will ask)

**1 Week Before:**
- [ ] Present to lab/peer group for feedback
- [ ] Revise based on feedback
- [ ] Print copies of proposal for committee (if not already provided)
- [ ] Confirm meeting logistics (time, location, Zoom link if remote)

**Day Before:**
- [ ] Get good sleep (more important than last-minute prep)
- [ ] Check all technical equipment (projector, slides, remote)
- [ ] Review opening remarks (should be smooth, conversational)

### 4.2 Defense Presentation Structure (15–20 minutes)

**1. Opening (1–2 minutes)**
- Brief greeting; state purpose ("Thank you for being here today. I'm excited to present my dissertation proposal on...")
- Overview of talk (what you'll cover, how long it will take)

**2. Problem & Significance (2–3 minutes)**
- Hook: Why should we care about this problem?
- Problem statement (concise; 1 minute)
- Significance: Who benefits from answering your RQs?

**3. Literature Summary (3–4 minutes)**
- Key domains: Show you know the field
- Gap: What's missing? (Lead into RQs)
- Avoid: Don't list every study. Synthesize.

**4. Research Questions (1 minute)**
- State clearly
- Explain: Why these questions? How do they flow from the gap?

**5. Methodology (4–5 minutes)**
- Design: What approach? Why?
- Sample: Who? How many? How recruited?
- Data collection: What data? How?
- Analysis: How will you answer RQs?
- Trustworthiness/Validity: How will you ensure rigor?

**6. Timeline (1–2 minutes)**
- Semester-by-semester (or month-by-month) breakdown
- Major milestones
- Feasibility statement

**7. Closing (1 minute)**
- Recap: "In sum, I plan to study X using Y methods with Z sample to understand..."
- Thank committee; open to questions

**Slide Tips:**
- 12–15 slides maximum (don't crowd)
- One key idea per slide
- Use visuals (diagrams, models, charts) where possible
- Large font (36pt minimum for body text)
- Avoid text-heavy slides; you provide the narrative

### 4.3 Anticipating Committee Questions

**Questions About Problem & Significance:**
- *"Why is this problem important now?"* 
  → Answer: Point to recent event, policy, data, or research that makes it timely
- *"Who specifically will benefit from this research?"*
  → Answer: Teachers (in inclusive schools), administrators, policymakers, students with disabilities, educational researchers
- *"How is this different from [related study X]?"*
  → Answer: Acknowledge related work; state your novel angle (different population, different method, different context, different RQ)

**Questions About Literature Review:**
- *"How does your theoretical framework shape your analysis?"*
  → Answer: Explain how the lens influences interpretation; give example of how it guides coding/analysis
- *"What's the gap you're addressing?"*
  → Answer: Articulate clearly: "We know A and B, but we don't know C—this study addresses C"
- *"Have you considered [competing theory/framework]?"*
  → Answer: Acknowledge; explain why your choice is better-fit for your RQs

**Questions About Methodology:**
- *"How will you ensure your sample is representative?"*
  → Answer: (Qualitative) "Purposeful sampling for diversity in X, Y, Z"; (Quantitative) "Random assignment; demographic representation"
- *"How many participants do you need?"*
  → Answer: Cite justification (saturation for qual; power analysis for quant)
- *"What if you can't recruit enough participants?"*
  → Answer: Have a Plan B (expand recruitment, adjust sample criteria, extend timeline)
- *"What's your coding scheme?"* (qualitative)
  → Answer: Describe inductive + deductive approach; reference your codebook
- *"What statistical tests will you use?"* (quantitative)
  → Answer: Name the tests; justify choices; explain assumptions
- *"How will you handle [specific methodological issue: missing data, attrition, sensitive questions]?"*
  → Answer: Specific mitigation strategy

**Questions About Feasibility:**
- *"Can you really collect all this data in 12 months?"*
  → Answer: Break down timeline; show experience/capacity (e.g., "I've conducted 10 interviews already as a pilot")
- *"Do you have access to participants/sites?"*
  → Answer: "I have verbal commitment from [school principal's name] to access teachers"; or "I'm in process of requesting access"
- *"What are your biggest challenges?"*
  → Answer: Name 1–2 real challenges; state mitigation; show you've thought this through

**Questions About Limitations:**
- *"What are this study's limitations?"*
  → Answer: Acknowledge upfront (sample size, generalizability, single context, etc.); explain why study is still valuable despite limitations
- *"How will these limitations affect your conclusions?"*
  → Answer: "These findings may not generalize to X context, so I'll be careful to frame conclusions for [target audience]"

### 4.4 Handling Tough Questions in the Defense

**If You Don't Know:**
- "That's a great question. I haven't thought through that yet. Could I get back to you on that?" (Then do.)
- "I'm not sure how to answer that. Can you help me think through it?" (Shows willingness to learn; invites collaboration)
- Don't make up answers; committees respect honest uncertainty.

**If You Disagree with Feedback:**
- Acknowledge the comment: "I hear your concern about..."
- Explain your reasoning: "I chose X approach because..."
- Offer to revisit: "That's worth reconsidering. I can explore that for the next version."
- You don't have to agree in the moment; you can revise after.

**If Someone Challenges Your Paradigm Choice (qual vs. quant):**
- Don't be defensive. State your case: "My RQs are exploratory/descriptive/interpretive, so qualitative [or quantitative] is the best fit because..."
- Acknowledge trade-offs: "There are trade-offs; quantitative would give me breadth, but I need depth to understand how and why..."

**If the Room Feels Adversarial:**
- Remember: Committee wants you to succeed. Hard questions are a sign they're invested.
- Stay calm and professional
- Answer the question; don't argue
- Thank them for pushing you to think harder

### 4.5 Post-Defense Revisions

**Expected Outcome:** Most proposals receive a "pass with revisions" decision

**Typical Revisions:**
- Strengthen literature review (add missing citation; clarify theoretical framework)
- Tighten methodology (specify sample size justification; add detail on data analysis)
- Clarify timeline (make feasible or extend)
- Refine RQs (more specific, better aligned)

**Revision Timeline:**
- Receive committee feedback: 1 week after defense
- Revise proposal: 2–4 weeks (depends on scope of changes)
- Resubmit to committee for final approval: 1 week
- Move to data collection

## Part 5: Proposal Defense Checklist

### Before Defense
- [ ] Proposal is well-written, error-free, formatted per requirements
- [ ] Distributed to committee ≥ 2 weeks before
- [ ] Read each committee member's recent publications
- [ ] 15-minute presentation prepared and practiced (out loud, timed)
- [ ] Slides created (12–15 slides, clear visuals)
- [ ] Anticipatory questions prepared (5–10 likely questions + answers drafted)
- [ ] 1-page executive summary created
- [ ] Timeline visual created
- [ ] Limitations identified (know what committees will ask)
- [ ] Feasibility statement thought through (access, timeline, resources)
- [ ] IRB/ethics considerations documented

### Day of Defense
- [ ] Arrive 10 minutes early; test technology
- [ ] Have printed copies of proposal if needed
- [ ] Have water nearby (dry mouth is common)
- [ ] Note pads in front; be ready to take notes on feedback
- [ ] Dress professionally
- [ ] Deep breathing before starting; speak clearly and confidently

### During Defense
- [ ] Maintain eye contact with committee
- [ ] Speak conversationally (not read off slides)
- [ ] Pause for questions; don't rush
- [ ] Listen fully before answering
- [ ] Thank questioner; acknowledge good points
- [ ] Show you've thought deeply about trade-offs

### After Defense
- [ ] Thank committee members individually
- [ ] Ask when to expect feedback
- [ ] Get clarification on revision expectations
- [ ] Schedule revision deadline with committee
- [ ] Complete revisions on timeline
- [ ] Resubmit revised proposal
- [ ] Once approved, move to IRB and data collection

## Summary

Your proposal is your research blueprint. Writing it forces you to think through every aspect of your study: what you're studying, why it matters, how you'll study it, and whether it's feasible. Defending it gives you the chance to show your committee—and yourself—that you're ready to do the work.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Clarity** — Be specific; avoid vagueness
2. **Coherence** — Everything should connect: problem → gap → RQs → methods
3. **Confidence** — Write with conviction; show you've thought this through
4. **Humility** — Acknowledge limitations; show openness to feedback
5. **Preparedness** — Anticipate questions; know your proposal inside-out

Your defense is not an interrogation; it's a conversation with people who want to help you succeed. Go in prepared, speak with confidence, and trust that your committee is on your side.
