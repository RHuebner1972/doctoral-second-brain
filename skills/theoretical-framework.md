# Theoretical & Conceptual Framework Builder

## Overview

The theoretical (or conceptual) framework is the intellectual scaffolding of your dissertation. It explains *why* you expect the relationships or experiences you're studying to exist. It anchors your study in established knowledge, justifies your design choices, and shapes your data collection and interpretation. This skill helps you select, explain, and apply the right framework.

---

## Theoretical vs. Conceptual Framework

These terms are often used interchangeably but have a meaningful distinction:

| | Theoretical Framework | Conceptual Framework |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | A single established theory from the literature | A researcher-constructed model combining multiple theories or concepts |
| **When to use** | When a well-validated theory directly explains your phenomenon | When no single theory fully captures the complexity of your study |
| **Example** | Self-Determination Theory, Social Cognitive Theory, Servant Leadership Theory | A model combining Bandura's self-efficacy + Kotter's change model + organizational learning theory |
| **How it's cited** | Cite the original theorist | Cite each component theory; explain the integration |

## Intake Questions

1. **What is your research topic / central phenomenon?**
2. **What are your key constructs or variables?** (e.g., servant leadership, retention, engagement)
3. **What methodology are you using?** (Theory functions differently in qualitative vs. quantitative work)
4. **Have you encountered any theories in your literature review that seem relevant?**
5. **What discipline are you in?** (Helps narrow the theory space)

## Framework Selection by Methodology

### Qualitative Studies
In qualitative work, the framework serves as a *lens* that shapes what you look for and how you interpret findings. It does not constrain the data — it orients the inquiry.

- **Interpretivism / Social Constructivism** (Berger & Luckmann, 1966; Vygotsky) — meaning is socially constructed
- **Phenomenology** often uses Husserl or Heidegger as the philosophical grounding
- **Critical theories** (Critical Race Theory, Feminist Theory) — examine power, oppression, voice
- **Organizational theories** — Sensemaking (Weick), Institutional Theory (DiMaggio & Powell)

### Quantitative Studies
In quantitative work, the framework generates *hypotheses* and predicts directional relationships. The theory explains *why* you expect Variable A to predict Variable B.

- Use the framework to justify which variables you selected and why
- The theory predicts the direction and mechanism of relationships
- Statistical models should mirror the theoretical model

### Mixed Methods
Use the framework to integrate the two strands — it explains what you're measuring quantitatively AND what you're exploring qualitatively.

## Common Frameworks by Domain

### Leadership & Organizational Studies
| Framework | Core Claim | Key Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Servant Leadership Theory | Leaders who serve followers first produce better outcomes | Greenleaf (1977); Liden et al. (2008) |
| Transformational Leadership | Leaders inspire followers to exceed expectations | Burns (1978); Bass (1985) |
| Self-Determination Theory | Intrinsic motivation requires autonomy, competence, relatedness | Deci & Ryan (1985) |
| Organizational Justice | Perceived fairness drives engagement and commitment | Greenberg (1987) |
| Social Exchange Theory | Relationships are reciprocal exchanges of valued resources | Blau (1964) |

### Education & Learning
| Framework | Core Claim | Key Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Adult Learning Theory (Andragogy) | Adults learn differently from children; self-directed | Knowles (1980) |
| Communities of Practice | Learning occurs through participation in social groups | Wenger (1998) |
| Transformative Learning | Learning occurs when assumptions are challenged | Mezirow (1991) |
| Social Cognitive Theory | Behavior is influenced by environment, self-efficacy, and cognition | Bandura (1977) |

### Healthcare & Nursing
| Framework | Core Claim | Key Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Health Belief Model | Health behavior driven by perceived susceptibility and benefit | Rosenstock (1966) |
| Theory of Planned Behavior | Intentions predict behavior; shaped by attitudes and norms | Ajzen (1985) |
| Transtheoretical Model | Behavior change occurs in stages | Prochaska & DiClemente (1983) |

### Social Sciences & Policy
| Framework | Core Claim | Key Theorists |
|---|---|---|
| Social Capital Theory | Networks of relationships produce resources and trust | Bourdieu (1986); Putnam (1995) |
| Institutional Theory | Organizations conform to social norms and expectations | DiMaggio & Powell (1983) |
| Critical Race Theory | Race is a social construct; racism is systemic | Crenshaw et al. (1995) |

## How to Write the Framework Section

The theoretical/conceptual framework section typically appears in Chapter 1 (overview) and Chapter 2 (full treatment). Use this structure:

```
1. Name and define the framework.
   "[Framework name] was developed by [Theorist] to explain [core claim]."

2. Describe its key components or propositions.
   "The theory proposes that [X], [Y], and [Z] are the primary drivers of [outcome]."

3. Summarize the empirical support.
   "The framework has been applied extensively in [domain], with studies demonstrating [key findings]."

4. Explain why it fits YOUR study.
   "This framework is appropriate for the current study because [specific connection
   to your phenomenon, population, or RQ]."

5. Explain how it will guide data collection and analysis.
   "Specifically, [framework component A] will inform [interview question / survey item /
   analysis approach]."
```

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] The framework is named and attributed to its originator(s)
- [ ] The key components of the framework are explained
- [ ] Empirical studies using this framework are cited
- [ ] There is a clear, explicit connection to YOUR research problem and questions
- [ ] The framework is present in Chapter 1 (briefly) and Chapter 2 (fully)
- [ ] Data collection instruments or analysis procedures connect to the framework
- [ ] Findings chapter uses framework language when interpreting results
