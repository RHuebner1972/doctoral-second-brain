# Research Questions Builder

## Overview

Research questions are the navigational spine of the dissertation. They convert the problem into something answerable. A central research question (CRQ) defines the scope of the entire study. Sub-questions break the CRQ into researchable components that the data can actually answer. Every design decision — sampling, instrumentation, analysis — flows downstream from the RQs. This skill generates and stress-tests your questions for alignment, answerability, and methodological fit.

---

## Intake Questions

1. **What is your problem statement or research gap?** (Paste it or summarize.)
2. **What is your methodology?** (qualitative, quantitative, mixed methods — and if known, the design: phenomenology, grounded theory, survey, quasi-experimental, case study, etc.)
3. **What phenomenon, experience, or relationship are you trying to understand?**
4. **Who are your participants or units of analysis?**
5. **What do you want to know that you don't already know?** (In plain language.)

## Qualitative Research Questions

### Characteristics of Strong Qualitative RQs
- Begin with *what*, *how*, or *why* (not *does*, *is there a difference*, or *to what extent*)
- Use the language of the methodology (phenomenology → *experience*; grounded theory → *process*; case study → *how/why*)
- Name the phenomenon and the population explicitly
- Are open-ended and exploratory — do not presuppose an outcome
- Cannot be answered with a yes/no

### Central Research Question Template (Qualitative)
```
What is/are [the phenomenon — experience, meaning, process, perspective] of
[population] in [context/setting]?
```

**Examples:**
- *What is the lived experience of first-generation doctoral students navigating imposter syndrome during the dissertation phase?*
- *How do community college faculty perceive servant leadership behaviors in their direct supervisors?*
- *What meaning do rural nurses ascribe to professional isolation in underserved settings?*

### Sub-Question Templates (Qualitative)
Sub-questions drill into specific dimensions of the central question:
```
SQ1: How do [participants] describe [specific aspect of the phenomenon]?
SQ2: What factors [shape / contribute to / influence] [the phenomenon] among [participants]?
SQ3: How do [participants] make sense of / respond to / navigate [the phenomenon]?
```

## Quantitative Research Questions

### Characteristics of Strong Quantitative RQs
- Identify variables explicitly (independent, dependent, and if applicable, moderating/mediating)
- Use precise language: *relationship*, *difference*, *effect*, *prediction*, *correlation*
- Name the population and the measurement instrument or construct
- Are answerable with a specific statistical test
- Match the level of measurement of the variables

### Central Research Question Template (Quantitative)
```
To what extent is there a [relationship / difference / effect] between
[independent variable(s)] and [dependent variable] among [population]?
```

**Examples:**
- *To what extent does transformational leadership predict organizational commitment among mid-level managers in the healthcare sector?*
- *Is there a statistically significant difference in employee engagement scores between teams with high vs. low psychological safety?*
- *To what extent do servant leadership behaviors, emotional intelligence, and tenure predict faculty retention intention in community colleges?*

### Hypotheses (if applicable)
For each quantitative RQ, pair it with a null and alternative hypothesis:
```
H₀: There is no statistically significant relationship between [X] and [Y] among [population].
H₁: There is a statistically significant relationship between [X] and [Y] among [population].
```

## Mixed Methods Research Questions

A mixed methods study requires three types of questions:
1. **Quantitative strand question** — answers the *what* and *how much*
2. **Qualitative strand question** — answers the *why* and *how*
3. **Mixed methods integration question** — addresses how the two strands connect

**Example:**
- *QUAN: To what extent do servant leadership behaviors predict faculty retention intention in community colleges?*
- *QUAL: How do community college faculty describe the influence of their supervisor's leadership style on their decision to stay or leave?*
- *MIXED: In what ways do the quantitative predictors of retention intention align with or diverge from faculty qualitative accounts of leadership influence?*

## RQ Quality Checklist

| Criterion | Qualitative | Quantitative |
|---|---|---|
| Names the population | ✓ | ✓ |
| Names the phenomenon / variables | ✓ | ✓ |
| Answerable with proposed method | ✓ | ✓ |
| Not already answered in the literature | ✓ | ✓ |
| Flows directly from the problem statement | ✓ | ✓ |
| Open-ended (qual) / testable (quant) | ✓ | ✓ |
| Appropriately scoped for one study | ✓ | ✓ |
| Free of jargon the committee will dispute | ✓ | ✓ |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| "Does leadership affect outcomes?" | Too vague. Name the specific leadership behavior and the specific outcome. |
| "Why is leadership important?" | Not researchable — this is a rhetorical question. |
| 7+ sub-questions | Limit to 3–5. Each sub-question implies data collection and analysis. |
| Sub-questions that are unrelated to the CRQ | Every sub-question must be a component of the central question. |
| Qualitative question starting with "Does…" | Qualitative RQs are exploratory. Use "What," "How," or "Why." |
| RQ that presupposes a finding | "How does leadership damage retention?" assumes damage. Use "influence" or "shape." |

---

## Alignment Check

Before finalizing, verify alignment across the dissertation spine:

```
Problem → Gap → Purpose → Central RQ → Sub-Questions → Method → Analysis
```

Each element should logically follow from the one before it. If the RQ doesn't connect cleanly to the problem, the study will struggle to justify itself.
