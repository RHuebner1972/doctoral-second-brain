# Argument Builder

## Purpose
Evaluate the logical structure of a scholarly argument and help the learner rebuild it into a coherent, well-evidenced chain of reasoning.

## What Makes a Scholarly Argument

A scholarly argument has:
1. **A clear claim** — a contestable proposition (not a fact)
2. **Evidence** — cited data, findings, or authoritative assertions that support the claim
3. **Reasoning** — the logical bridge between evidence and claim (the "therefore")
4. **Acknowledgment of counterarguments** — shows awareness of competing views
5. **Conclusion** — restates the claim in light of the evidence

## The Toulmin Model (Core Framework)

| Element | Question | Role |
|---------|----------|------|
| **Claim** | What are you arguing? | The position |
| **Data/Evidence** | What evidence supports it? | The grounds |
| **Warrant** | Why does the evidence support the claim? | The reasoning bridge |
| **Backing** | What supports the warrant? | Added support |
| **Qualifier** | How certain is this claim? | The hedge |
| **Rebuttal** | What would challenge this, and how do you address it? | The counterargument |

## Paragraph-Level Argument Structure (PEEL)

Each paragraph in a dissertation should follow this pattern:

- **P — Point:** Topic sentence that states the argument of the paragraph
- **E — Evidence:** 2–3 citations supporting the point
- **E — Explanation:** How the evidence supports the point (don't leave it to the reader)
- **L — Link:** Transition connecting to the next paragraph or building toward the larger argument

## Process

### Step 1 — Receive the Argument
Ask the learner to:
1. State their main claim (in one sentence if possible)
2. Share the passage or argument they want evaluated
3. Identify where they feel it's weakest

### Step 2 — Diagnose the Argument

**Check for claim clarity:**
- Is there a clear, contestable claim? Or is it a description/summary?
- Is the claim specific enough to be supported or challenged?

**Check evidence quality:**
- Is evidence cited?
- Are sources recent and credible?
- Is there enough evidence (3+ sources for major claims)?
- Is evidence from a single source (vulnerability)?

**Check the reasoning:**
- Is the connection between evidence and claim explicit?
- Does the evidence actually support this claim, or is the learner assuming the reader will connect the dots?

**Check for logical fallacies:**
- **Overgeneralization:** "All leaders in this sector..."
- **False causation:** "X happened, then Y happened, therefore X caused Y"
- **Appeal to authority without evidence:** "Scholars agree that..."
- **Circular reasoning:** "This is true because this is true"
- **Strawman:** Misrepresenting a counterargument to dismiss it easily

**Check the counterargument:**
- Is an opposing view acknowledged?
- Is it addressed substantively or dismissed?

### Step 3 — Rebuild the Argument
Return a restructured version:
1. Revised claim (sharper, more specific)
2. Evidence (organized, with citation placeholders)
3. Explicit reasoning (the "because" statement)
4. Counterargument acknowledgment
5. Conclusion statement

### Step 4 — Write the Revised Passage
Draft the argument as a paragraph or section, ready to paste into the dissertation.

## Output Format
1. **Argument diagnosis** (what's working, what's broken)
2. **Toulmin map** of the argument (claim, data, warrant, rebuttal)
3. **Logical issues identified** (with specific examples from the text)
4. **Revised argument structure** (outline form)
5. **Draft revised passage** (paragraph-ready)
6. **Recommended sources to add** (if evidence is thin)
