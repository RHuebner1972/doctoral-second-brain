# Theoretical / Conceptual Framework

## Purpose
Help the learner identify the right theoretical or conceptual framework, explain it accurately, and apply it explicitly to anchor every aspect of the dissertation.

## Theoretical vs. Conceptual Framework — The Distinction

| | Theoretical Framework | Conceptual Framework |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | An established theory from the literature | Assembled by the researcher from multiple theories/concepts |
| **Function** | Applies a pre-existing explanatory lens | Maps the relationships between constructs specific to this study |
| **Common in** | Studies testing or extending a theory | Exploratory studies, applied research, mixed methods |
| **Example** | Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory | A custom model linking leadership style → psychological safety → performance |

Many dissertations use both: a theoretical framework anchors the study philosophically, and a conceptual framework maps the specific variables or themes.

## Process

### Step 1 — Identify Candidate Frameworks
Ask the learner:
1. What is the core phenomenon you are studying?
2. What theories have other scholars used to study this or related phenomena?
3. What constructs are central to your research questions?

Search these sources of frameworks:
- The literature review (what theories did cited studies use?)
- Adjacent fields (psychology, sociology, organizational theory, education)
- Classic and foundational works in the discipline

### Step 2 — Evaluate Each Candidate
For each candidate theory, assess:
- **Fit:** Does it explain the phenomenon you're studying?
- **Testability:** Can your research questions be derived from its propositions?
- **Precedent:** Has it been used in similar studies?
- **Scope:** Is it appropriately broad or specific for your study?

### Step 3 — Select and Justify
Choose the framework that best fits and provide a 3-part justification:
1. What the theory proposes (its core claims)
2. How it has been used in prior research (2–3 citations)
3. Why it is the appropriate lens for THIS study

### Step 4 — Apply the Framework
The framework must do more than appear in Chapter 2 — it must:
- Inform the research questions (questions derived from framework propositions)
- Shape the methodology (the framework determines what counts as valid knowledge)
- Structure data collection (instruments operationalize framework constructs)
- Organize the analysis (themes/variables align with framework elements)
- Anchor the discussion (findings are interpreted through the framework)

### Step 5 — Write the Framework Section
Structure:
1. **Introduction** — why a theoretical framework is needed and what it does
2. **Overview of the framework** — origin, key theorist(s), core propositions
3. **Prior application** — how others have applied it (2–3 studies)
4. **Application to this study** — explicit connections to your RQs, design, and constructs
5. **Visual model (optional)** — a diagram showing how constructs relate

## Common Frameworks by Domain

**Leadership / Organizational:**
- Transformational Leadership Theory (Bass & Avolio)
- Servant Leadership Theory (Greenleaf, Spears)
- Social Exchange Theory (Blau)
- Organizational Learning Theory (Argyris & Schön)

**Education / Learning:**
- Constructivism (Vygotsky, Piaget)
- Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)
- Communities of Practice (Lave & Wenger)
- Adult Learning Theory / Andragogy (Knowles)

**Social / Behavioral:**
- Social Cognitive Theory (Bandura)
- Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen)
- Ecological Systems Theory (Bronfenbrenner)

**Critical / Identity:**
- Critical Race Theory (Crenshaw, Bell)
- Feminist Theory
- Intersectionality (Crenshaw)

## Output Format
1. **Recommended framework(s)** (with rationale)
2. **Framework comparison** (if multiple were considered)
3. **Draft framework section** (400–600 words, APA citations, ready for Chapter 2)
4. **Application map** — how the framework connects to RQs, methodology, data collection, and analysis
5. **Visual model description** (text description of a diagram the learner can draw)
