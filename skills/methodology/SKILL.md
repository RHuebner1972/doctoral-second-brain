# Methodology Selection and Justification

## Purpose
Guide the learner to select the most appropriate research methodology for their study and write a compelling, logically grounded methodological justification.

## The Core Logic
Methodology must follow from the research questions — not the other way around. The question to always ask:
> "What kind of knowledge am I trying to produce?"

## Decision Framework

### Level 1 — Research Paradigm
Identify the epistemological stance:

| Paradigm | Assumption | Typical Approach |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Positivism** | One objective reality; measured and tested | Quantitative |
| **Interpretivism** | Reality is socially constructed; multiple perspectives | Qualitative |
| **Pragmatism** | What works best to answer the question | Mixed Methods |
| **Critical** | Reality is shaped by power; research should challenge it | Critical Qualitative |

### Level 2 — Research Approach
Choose based on what the research questions seek:

**Qualitative** — if the questions ask *what*, *how*, or *why* about lived experience, meaning, or process
**Quantitative** — if the questions ask *how much*, *to what extent*, or *what is the relationship/difference*
**Mixed Methods** — if neither approach alone is sufficient

### Level 3 — Research Design (Tradition/Tradition)

**Qualitative Designs:**
| Design | Best For | Core Question Type |
|--------|----------|--------------------|
| **Phenomenology** | Lived experience of a phenomenon | "What is the essence of X for people who have experienced it?" |
| **Grounded Theory** | Developing theory from data | "What theory emerges from the experiences of X?" |
| **Case Study** | In-depth, bounded context | "How does X play out in this specific case?" |
| **Narrative Inquiry** | Individual stories and identity | "How does X shape the life narrative of Y?" |
| **Ethnography** | Culture and shared practices | "What is the culture of X group?" |

**Quantitative Designs:**
| Design | Best For | Core Question Type |
|--------|----------|--------------------|
| **Descriptive Survey** | Describing characteristics of a population | "What are the X patterns among Y?" |
| **Correlational** | Relationships between variables | "What is the relationship between X and Y?" |
| **Causal-Comparative (ex post facto)** | Differences between existing groups | "Is there a difference in X between Group A and Group B?" |
| **Experimental / Quasi-Experimental** | Causal effects with manipulation | "Does intervention X cause change in Y?" |

**Mixed Methods Designs:**
| Design | When to Use |
|--------|------------|
| **Explanatory Sequential** | Quantitative first, then qualitative to explain results |
| **Exploratory Sequential** | Qualitative first, then quantitative to test/generalize |
| **Convergent Parallel** | Both simultaneously, then merge findings |
| **Embedded** | One strand nested inside the other |

## Process

### Step 1 — Anchor in the Research Questions
List each research question. For each, identify:
- What type of knowledge does this question seek?
- What data could answer it?
- What analysis would make sense?

### Step 2 — Select Paradigm, Approach, and Design
Use the framework above. Make the selection explicitly, don't just land on one without justifying the alternatives.

### Step 3 — Write the Justification
Structure:
1. Restate the purpose of the study and research questions
2. Identify the paradigm and its assumptions (cite Creswell, or Merriam, or Guba & Lincoln)
3. Name the approach (qualitative/quantitative/mixed) and justify why
4. Name the specific design and justify why (cite the design's canonical source — e.g., Moustakas for phenomenology, Yin for case study, Glaser & Strauss for grounded theory)
5. Briefly address why alternative designs were not chosen

### Step 4 — Align With the Rest of Chapter 3
The methodology justification should explicitly connect:
- Problem statement → methodology
- Research questions → design choice
- Design → data collection strategy
- Data collection → analysis approach

## Common Errors to Flag
- Choosing qualitative because "I don't like statistics" (not a justification)
- Using grounded theory when a theory already exists (wrong design)
- Calling something a case study when there is no bounded case
- Using phenomenology with more than 10–15 participants (scope creep)
- Claiming mixed methods without true integration of strands

## Output Format
1. **Recommended paradigm, approach, and design** (with rationale for each)
2. **Design comparison** (why this over alternatives)
3. **Draft methodology justification paragraph** (200–300 words, APA citations)
4. **Canonical sources to cite** for the chosen design
5. **Alignment check** — research questions → design → data → analysis
6. **Next step** — participant selection, instrumentation, or data analysis plan
