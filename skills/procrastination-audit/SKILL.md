# Procrastination Audit

## Purpose
Diagnose *why* the learner is avoiding dissertation work — not just that they are — and prescribe a targeted, low-friction action that removes the specific barrier.

## The Key Insight
Procrastination is almost never about laziness. It is almost always about one of these:

1. **Ambiguity** — Not knowing exactly what to do next
2. **Fear** — Anxiety about being judged, being wrong, or not being good enough
3. **Overwhelm** — The task feels too large to begin
4. **Perfectionism** — Unwilling to produce a bad draft
5. **Disconnection** — The task doesn't feel meaningful in this moment
6. **Genuine exhaustion** — The tank is empty (addressed in `motivation-reset`)

## The Diagnostic Process

### Step 1 — Identify the Behavior
Ask the learner:
1. "What specific task are you avoiding?"
2. "How long have you been avoiding this particular task?"
3. "When you think about doing it, what do you feel?" (anxiety, boredom, confusion, dread)
4. "What do you do instead?" (scroll, read other things, do housework)

### Step 2 — Identify the Root Cause

**Diagnostic questions by suspected cause:**

**Is it ambiguity?**
> "If I sat you down right now with no distractions, would you know exactly what to type first?"
- Yes → not ambiguity; go deeper
- No → this is ambiguity

**Is it fear/perfectionism?**
> "Are you worried that what you write won't be good enough?"
> "Are you imagining your chair's reaction while you write?"
- Thinking about the audience while writing → perfectionism
- Worried about being "found out" → imposter syndrome (see `motivation-reset`)

**Is it overwhelm?**
> "Does the task feel too big to know where to start?"
> "Does thinking about the dissertation make you think of everything you haven't done?"
- Yes → overwhelm

**Is it disconnection?**
> "Does this section feel pointless or irrelevant right now?"
> "Are you writing something you don't actually believe in?"
- Yes → disconnection (may need a purpose check or a topic conversation)

### Step 3 — Prescribe by Cause

**Ambiguity → Clarify the next action**
The fix is specificity. Co-create the exact next sentence or section:
> "What you need to write is the opening sentence of the trustworthiness section. It sounds like: 'To establish the credibility of this study, the researcher employed member checking, peer debriefing, and prolonged engagement...'"

Prescribe: Open the document. Write that sentence. Nothing else is required today.

---

**Fear / Perfectionism → Lower the stakes**
The fix is permission to be bad:
> "Your job right now is not to write well. Your job is to produce a draft that your future self can edit."

Tactics:
- Change the font to 8pt gray — bad writing feels less real
- Write in a temporary scratch document, not the dissertation itself
- Set a timer for 10 minutes and write as badly as necessary
- Remind them: "No one reads the first draft except you."

---

**Overwhelm → Decompose the task**
The fix is to make the task smaller until it is undeniable:

> "You don't need to write Chapter 3. You need to write the opening paragraph of the methodology justification. That's one paragraph. That's it."

Break down: What is the one paragraph? What is the one sentence? What is the one word?

---

**Disconnection → Find the entry point**
The fix is to start with the part that doesn't feel empty:
> "Which part of your study do you still find interesting?"
> "If you could write about one thing today, what would it be?"

Start there, even if it's not the "right" section. Momentum is the priority.

### Step 4 — Commit to One Action
End every procrastination audit with a specific, time-bound commitment:
> "In the next [X minutes / by [time today]], I will [specific action]."

Examples:
> "In the next 15 minutes, I will write the first paragraph of the data analysis section."
> "By 5pm today, I will have opened the document and written at least one sentence."

### Step 5 — Remove the Obstacle
Ask: "What would make it easier to start?"
- Clear desk?
- Change location?
- Silence phone?
- Make coffee first?
- Put on focus music?

Remove one obstacle right now, before ending the conversation.

## The Five-Minute Rule
If all else fails: commit to five minutes. Just five. Then stop if you want.
The research on habit formation is clear: starting is the hardest part. Five minutes almost always becomes twenty.

## Output Format
1. **Root cause diagnosis** (ambiguity / fear / overwhelm / perfectionism / disconnection)
2. **Evidence for the diagnosis** (what the learner said that points to this cause)
3. **Targeted prescription** (specific to the diagnosed cause)
4. **The one action** (specific, time-bound, undeniable)
5. **Obstacle removed** (what friction was cleared)
6. **Check-in prompt** — a question the learner can ask themselves after the session
