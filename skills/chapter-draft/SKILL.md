# Chapter Draft

## Purpose
Produce a scholarly, dissertation-quality draft of any chapter section, tailored to the learner's topic, methodology, and research questions.

## Before Drafting — Required Inputs

Always ask for the following before generating a draft:

1. **Which chapter and section** are we drafting?
2. **What is the study topic and problem statement?** (or a summary)
3. **What are the research questions?**
4. **What methodology and design is being used?**
5. **What sources or content should be incorporated?** (paste in, summarize, or list)
6. **What is the target length?**
7. **Are there institutional template requirements?** (Walden, GCU, Northcentral, etc.)

## Chapter-Specific Drafting Guidelines

### Chapter 1 — Introduction

**Background of the Problem:**
- Start broad, narrow to the specific problem
- Each paragraph should move the reader closer to the problem statement
- Every claim must be cited
- Avoid opinion; use evidence and expert voices

**Problem Statement:**
- 1–2 paragraphs; grounded in evidence
- Name who is affected and what the consequence is
- Do not propose a solution

**Purpose Statement:**
- One sentence for qualitative: "The purpose of this [design] study was to [explore/examine/understand] [phenomenon] for [population] in [context]."
- One sentence for quantitative: "The purpose of this [design] study was to [examine/determine/test] the [relationship/difference/effect] between [variables] for [population]."

**Nature of the Study:**
- Briefly name the methodology, design, and rationale in 2–3 paragraphs
- Save the full justification for Chapter 3

### Chapter 2 — Literature Review

**Theme Section:**
- Open with a clear topic sentence naming the theme
- Build the argument using 4–6+ sources synthesized together
- Identify areas of agreement, disagreement, and evolution
- Close with a transition that builds toward the gap

**Theoretical Framework Section:**
- Name the theory and its origin
- Describe core propositions
- Cite prior applications
- Explicitly connect to this study's RQs and design

### Chapter 3 — Methodology

**Role of the Researcher:**
- Write in first person (exception: some institutions require third)
- Disclose relevant positionality
- Describe how bias was acknowledged and bracketed

**Data Collection:**
- Be specific: instruments, procedures, timeline, logistics
- Cite the instrument source and any pilot testing
- Describe how ethical protections were applied

### Chapter 4 — Findings

**By Research Question:**
- Open each section by restating the RQ
- Present themes/categories with evidence (participant quotes for qualitative; tables/statistics for quantitative)
- Label themes and give each a frequency or representative quote count
- Connect to the data, not to the literature (that's Chapter 5)

### Chapter 5 — Discussion

**Interpretation Section:**
- Connect each finding to the literature reviewed in Chapter 2
- Use language of comparison: "consistent with," "extends," "challenges," "corroborates"
- Interpret through the theoretical framework

**Recommendations for Practice:**
- Be specific and actionable
- Direct to specific audiences (leaders, educators, policymakers)
- Ground each in the findings

## Scholarly Register Checklist
Before finalizing any draft, verify:
- [ ] No first-person in lit review or methodology (unless required)
- [ ] Every empirical claim is cited
- [ ] Hedged language used appropriately ("may," "suggests," "appears")
- [ ] No informal language, contractions, or colloquialisms
- [ ] Transitions between paragraphs are explicit and logical
- [ ] Section closes with a forward-pointing summary or transition

## Output Format
1. **Draft section** (ready to paste into the dissertation)
2. **[CITATION NEEDED]** placeholders where learner must supply a source
3. **Draft notes** (callouts for sections that need expansion or verification)
4. **Word count**
5. **Next section** recommendation
