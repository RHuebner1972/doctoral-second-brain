# Interdisciplinary Bridging: Integrating Across Fields & Literatures

Interdisciplinary research is increasingly valued—it combines strengths of multiple fields to address complex problems. But it's also challenging: disciplines have different languages, assumptions, methodologies, and publication norms. This skill guides you through bridging disciplines intentionally, translating concepts across fields, and positioning work that legitimately integrates multiple perspectives.

## Part 1: Clarifying Your Interdisciplinary Position

### 1.1 Types of Interdisciplinary Work

**Multidisciplinary** (parallel; not integrated)
- Brings perspectives from multiple fields to bear on same problem
- But perspectives remain separate; no deep integration
- Example: Education + psychology + sociology each study teacher burnout separately; findings reported in parallel
- Contribution: Comprehensive view from multiple angles
- Publication: May publish separate papers in each field

**Interdisciplinary** (integrated; new synthesis)
- Combines concepts, methods, or theories from multiple fields into new synthesis
- Creates something that wouldn't exist in single discipline alone
- Example: Uses educational psychology + organizational theory + neuroscience to understand learning culture in schools
- Contribution: New theoretical integration; novel research questions
- Publication: Single integrated paper; may be challenge finding home

**Transdisciplinary** (integrated + problem-centered)
- Multiple disciplines integrated around real-world problem (not discipline questions)
- Driven by problem, not by disciplines
- Example: Climate adaptation problem drives integration of environmental science + economics + education + policy + community engagement
- Contribution: Solution-oriented; stakeholder-informed
- Publication: May be policy briefs, community reports, interdisciplinary journals

**Clarify Which Type is Your Work:**
- [ ] Multidisciplinary: Multiple disciplines, separate contributions
- [ ] Interdisciplinary: Integrated, creating synthesis
- [ ] Transdisciplinary: Problem-centered, stakeholder-informed

### 1.2 Identify Your Disciplinary Home vs. Borrowed Perspectives

**Most doctoral dissertations:**
- Home discipline: Where your degree is (Education, Psychology, Sociology, etc.)
- Borrowed: 1–2 other disciplines informing your perspective
- Example: PhD in Education, drawing from organizational psychology + educational sociology

**Why This Matters:**
- Your dissertation format, standards, and defense expectations come from home discipline
- But theoretical framework, methods, or findings may draw on other fields
- You need to be bilingual: speak home discipline language + translate across disciplinary boundaries

**Mapping Your Disciplines:**

```
HOME DISCIPLINE: Educational Leadership
├─ Primary theories: Distributed leadership, instructional leadership, 
│  transformational leadership
├─ Primary methods: Case study, ethnography, surveys
├─ Key journals: Educational Leadership Quarterly, AERA, Ed Admin Quarterly

DISCIPLINE 2: Organizational Psychology
├─ Related concepts: Team efficacy, organizational culture, change management
├─ Methods: Surveys, experimental designs, longitudinal studies
├─ Key journals: Journal of Applied Psychology, Organizational Science

DISCIPLINE 3: Sociology
├─ Related concepts: Social capital, institutional structures, power dynamics
├─ Methods: Ethnography, interviews, mixed methods
├─ Key journals: Sociology of Education, American Journal of Sociology

SYNTHESIS QUESTION:
How do these disciplines combine to address my RQ about teacher leadership 
in inclusive schools?
→ Education provides primary framework (what is teacher leadership?)
→ Org psych offers constructs (team efficacy, organizational culture)
→ Sociology offers lens (power, inequality, institutional structures)
```

## Part 2: Building Your Interdisciplinary Literature Review

### 2.1 Searching Across Disciplines

**The Challenge:** Different disciplines use different terms for similar concepts

**Example - "Teacher Leadership":**
- Education: "teacher leadership," "teacher activism," "teacher agency"
- Organizational psych: "team leadership," "distributed leadership," "informal leadership"
- Sociology: "grassroots organizing," "collective efficacy," "institutional authority"
- Management: "lateral leadership," "peer influence," "informal influence"

**Search Strategy:**
1. Identify your core concept
2. List terms used in home discipline
3. Find equivalent terms in other disciplines
4. Search each database (ERIC, PsycINFO, Sociological Abstracts, etc.) with discipline-specific terms
5. Compile results; look for overlaps

**Example Search Protocol:**

```
CORE CONCEPT: Teacher Leadership Development

Database: ERIC (Education)
  Search terms: "teacher leadership," "teacher activism," "teacher empowerment"
  Result: 342 articles
  Selection: Focus on "professional development" + "leadership"
  
Database: PsycINFO (Psychology)
  Search terms: "distributed leadership," "team leadership," "informal leadership"
  Result: 128 articles
  Selection: Focus on "development" + "teachers" or "school personnel"
  
Database: Sociological Abstracts (Sociology)
  Search terms: "teacher voice," "collective efficacy," "grassroots organizing"
  Result: 67 articles
  Selection: Focus on organizational/institutional aspects
  
INTEGRATION:
Compile all results. Look for articles citing across fields (indicators 
of already-integrated work). Identify core themes that emerge across fields.
```

### 2.2 Translating Concepts Across Disciplines

**The central task: What does concept X look like in discipline A vs. B?**

**Example - "Self-Efficacy":**

| Discipline | Definition | Measurement | Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Psychology** (Bandura) | Belief in capability to perform specific task | Self-report scale (Likert); outcome expectancy measures | Clinical, educational, organizational contexts |
| **Education** | Teacher confidence in ability to teach specific subject/skill | Teacher Efficacy Scale; classroom observation; practice changes | Predicts teacher behavior, student outcomes |
| **Organizational Studies** | Team or collective belief in capability to accomplish organizational goals | Team efficacy scale; team performance data | Predicts team performance, innovation, resilience |

**When Building Interdisciplinary Literature Review:**

For each major concept, create a translation table:

```
CONCEPT: Inclusive Teaching Capacity

DISCIPLINE: Education
  Definition: Teacher's knowledge, skills, and dispositions to differentiate 
  instruction for diverse learners
  Key theorists: Tomlinson, Wiggins & McTighe, Banks
  Measurement: Practice observations, student work analysis, teacher self-report
  Related terms: "differentiation," "responsive teaching," "culturally responsive teaching"

DISCIPLINE: Psychology (Individual)
  Definition: Teacher self-efficacy for inclusive teaching
  Key theorists: Bandura (self-efficacy), Tschannen-Moran (teacher efficacy)
  Measurement: Self-report efficacy scale; outcome expectancy
  Related terms: "self-efficacy," "confidence," "competence belief"

DISCIPLINE: Organizational Psychology / Sociology
  Definition: School organizational capacity for supporting diverse students
  Key theorists: Spillane (distributed leadership), Bryk (school improvement)
  Measurement: School survey data, organizational culture assessment, teacher survey
  Related terms: "organizational capacity," "collective efficacy," "school improvement"

INTEGRATION OPPORTUNITY:
These three perspectives (individual, group, organizational) are complementary.
Individual teacher efficacy is necessary but insufficient—organizational 
capacity matters too. An integrated framework would examine how both 
individual and organizational factors contribute to inclusive teaching.
```

### 2.3 Writing Your Interdisciplinary Literature Review

**Structure:** Instead of single literature review, consider nested structure:

```
II. LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 DOMAIN 1: INCLUSIVE EDUCATION POLICY & PRACTICE
  (Education discipline - primary home base)
  → What does research in education show about inclusive education?
  → What are barriers and enablers?

2.2 DOMAIN 2: TEACHER LEARNING & PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT
  (Educational psychology + pedagogy)
  → What does research show about how teachers learn?
  → What PD approaches are most effective?

2.3 DOMAIN 3: ORGANIZATIONAL LEADERSHIP & CHANGE
  (Organizational psychology + management)
  → How do organizations change?
  → What leadership practices support change?

2.4 DOMAIN 4: SOCIAL STRUCTURES & POWER
  (Sociology)
  → How do organizational structures and power dynamics shape 
    teaching and learning?
  → How do inequities persist despite policy?

2.5 SYNTHESIS: INTEGRATING PERSPECTIVES
  → How do these domains intersect?
  → Where are tensions? Complementarities?
  → This is where your new synthesis emerges
```

**Writing Synthesizing Paragraphs (The Glue):**

When you integrate across domains, write paragraphs that explicitly connect:

```
Individual teacher efficacy is necessary but not sufficient for 
inclusive teaching. Educational research shows that confident teachers 
implement more differentiated instruction (Tschannen-Moran & Woolfolk Hoy, 2001). 
Yet organizational psychology research shows that individual-level 
changes are constrained by organizational culture and structures 
(Bryk et al., 2010; Spillane, 2006). When schools lack shared beliefs 
about inclusion or allocate resources toward segregation, individual 
teachers' efficacy may translate into frustration rather than practice 
change. This suggests an integrated framework: teacher learning (psychology 
domain) is shaped by organizational context (organizational psychology / 
sociology domains). Professional development must address both individual 
learning AND organizational conditions for change to be sustained.
```

**Notice the Moves:**
1. Educational finding (individual efficacy matters)
2. Organizational finding (context matters)
3. Tension/limitation identified
4. Integration proposed (both matter; one influences other)

## Part 3: Translating for Different Audiences

### 3.1 Disciplinary Language Differences

**Same Concept, Different Words:**

| Concept | Education | Psychology | Sociology | Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "Change" | Curriculum reform, implementation | Behavioral change, learning | Social change, structural change | Organizational change, transformation |
| "Teacher Growth" | Professional development, instructional improvement | Individual learning, skill acquisition | Consciousness-raising, empowerment | Competency development, employee development |
| "Leadership" | Instructional leadership, teacher leadership, distributed leadership | Influence, informal power | Authority, legitimacy, power relations | Formal & informal leadership, followership |
| "Success" | Student learning gains, achievement | Behavior change, efficacy, outcome | Equity, social justice, participation | Organizational goals, performance |

**When Writing for Mixed Audience:**
- [ ] Define key terms (don't assume shared meaning across disciplines)
- [ ] Use accessible language (avoid discipline-specific jargon when possible)
- [ ] Translate key concepts (explain how concept in one field relates to another)
- [ ] Show your work (make your interdisciplinary move explicit, not hidden)

### 3.2 Acknowledging Disciplinary Tensions

**Different disciplines may have different assumptions:**

| Discipline | Assumption | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Psychology** | Individual differences matter most; individual change is primary unit | Emphasis on individual teacher development |
| **Sociology** | Social structures shape behavior; focus on institutions & power | Emphasis on organizational/systemic barriers & change |
| **Education** | Context matters; multiple factors interact; practical outcomes matter | Emphasis on "what works" in real schools; multiple levers for change |
| **Economics** | Rational actors; cost-benefit analysis; scalability matters | Emphasis on ROI, sustainability, efficiency |

**When Different Disciplines Conflict:**

Instead of choosing one, acknowledge both:

```
DIFFERENT PERSPECTIVE ON PD EFFECTIVENESS:

Psychology perspective: PD is most effective when it builds individual 
teacher knowledge, skills, and confidence (efficacy). Therefore, high-quality 
content delivery, active learning strategies, and follow-up support matter most.

Sociology perspective: PD's impact is limited by organizational context. 
If school culture doesn't support change, or if structures actively prevent 
implementation, individual teacher learning won't translate to practice change. 
Therefore, organizational change (culture, structures, incentives) may matter 
more than content delivery.

Integrated perspective: Both matter. Teacher learning is necessary but 
not sufficient. PD must be designed WITH attention to organizational context 
AND organizations must create conditions for teacher learning to translate 
to practice. This requires viewing PD as embedded in organizational change, 
not separate from it.
```

## Part 4: Positioning Your Interdisciplinary Contribution

### 4.1 Articulating the "So What?" of Integration

**Why does your interdisciplinary approach matter more than single-discipline approach?**

**Make the case:**

```
SINGLE-DISCIPLINE APPROACHES & LIMITATIONS:

Education perspective alone: Offers rich understanding of inclusive teaching 
practices and barriers but may underestimate organizational constraints. 
Could lead to PD that teaches practices teachers can't actually implement 
given school structures and culture.

Psychology perspective alone: Offers understanding of individual learning and 
efficacy but may ignore systemic barriers. Could overestimate individual 
agency and underestimate power of organizational structures.

Sociology perspective alone: Offers critical analysis of power and structures 
but may miss opportunities for individual and team-level change. Could lead 
to fatalism ("nothing matters except macro-level change").

INTERDISCIPLINARY ADVANTAGE:

An integrated approach examines how individual learning, team efficacy, and 
organizational structures interact to enable or constrain inclusive teaching. 
This reveals both leverage points for change (where individual, team, and 
organizational levels could align to support change) and barriers (where 
misalignment prevents implementation). It offers a more complete picture 
of what supports or constrains inclusive teaching implementation, leading 
to more effective interventions.
```

### 4.2 Addressing Potential Criticism

**Interdisciplinary work can face criticism from single-discipline perspectives:**

| Criticism | Response |
|---|---|
| "You're not rigorous in any one discipline" | "Disciplinary rigor is maintained; integration adds comprehensiveness. I use discipline-specific standards for each perspective." |
| "This isn't really education research / psychology / sociology" | "You're right; it's education research informed by psychology & sociology. My home discipline is [X]; I borrow from others to strengthen the work." |
| "You can't possibly know multiple disciplines well" | "I'm not claiming expertise across all fields. I'm showing how key concepts from other disciplines illuminate my home-discipline questions. I cite field experts in each area." |
| "Your work is scattered; lacks coherence" | "The integration is intentional; here's the logic connecting them [explain synthesis]. Single discipline alone would miss this." |

## Part 5: Methods & Interdisciplinary Research

### 5.1 Choosing Methods Across Disciplines

**Different disciplines favor different methodologies:**

| Discipline | Primary Methods | Epistemology |
|---|---|---|
| **Psychology** | Experiments, surveys, outcome studies | Positivist; seek generalizable laws |
| **Education** | Mixed methods, case studies, design research | Pragmatic; seek what works in context |
| **Sociology** | Ethnography, interviews, critical analysis | Interpretivist, critical; seek to understand power & meaning |
| **Management** | Surveys, longitudinal studies, mixed methods | Pragmatic; seek to improve organizational effectiveness |

**Your Choice:** What method is best for your RQs?

- If exploring mechanisms (how X leads to Y): Qualitative (ethnography, interviews)
- If comparing interventions: Experimental or quasi-experimental
- If understanding experiences: Phenomenology or narrative
- If examining structure: Mixed methods or critical ethnography
- If scaling findings: Survey or experimental

**Bridging Methodologies:**
- Use qualitative to understand context & mechanism (sociology/education contribution)
- Use quantitative to assess individual efficacy & outcomes (psychology contribution)
- Integrate through sequential or concurrent mixed methods

## Part 6: Interdisciplinary Dissertation Checklist

### Before Writing Proposal
- [ ] Disciplines identified (home + borrowed fields)
- [ ] Clarified: multidisciplinary vs. interdisciplinary vs. transdisciplinary
- [ ] Key concept translation table created (same idea, different words)
- [ ] Literature searched across disciplines (ERIC + PsycINFO + others)
- [ ] Disciplinary tensions identified and acknowledged
- [ ] Integration argument articulated (why this combination is meaningful)
- [ ] Methods selected based on RQs (not just home-discipline default)

### In Writing Proposal
- [ ] Problem statement framed in way that shows why integration needed
- [ ] Literature review organized by domains + synthesis section connecting them
- [ ] Theoretical framework explicitly integrates perspectives
- [ ] RQs are interdisciplinary (not just one-discipline questions)
- [ ] Methods justified (why this approach answers interdisciplinary RQs?)
- [ ] "So what?" of integration is clear (why does this matter?)

### In Dissertation Chapters
- [ ] Ch. 2: Seamlessly integrates literatures (not parallel sections)
- [ ] Key concepts defined consistently across disciplines
- [ ] Disciplinary tensions acknowledged and addressed
- [ ] Theoretical framework visually represented showing integration
- [ ] Findings interpreted through multiple lenses
- [ ] Discussion addresses implications across disciplinary boundaries

### In Defense/Publication
- [ ] Can articulate to home-discipline audience why integration strengthens work
- [ ] Can articulate to other-discipline audience what you're contributing
- [ ] Acknowledge disciplinary home (you're education researcher informed by psych + sociology)
- [ ] Claim your integration as contribution (not apologize for it)

## Summary

Interdisciplinary research is increasingly necessary for complex problems—and increasingly rewarded. But it requires intentionality: clarifying which disciplines you're integrating, learning to translate across fields, acknowledging tensions, and making explicit the case for integration.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Clarity** — Be clear about home discipline + borrowed fields
2. **Translation** — Same concept, different words; learn the translation
3. **Integration, not Parallel** — Don't just list perspectives; show how they interact
4. **Acknowledge Tensions** — Different disciplines may disagree; that's okay
5. **Justify the Integration** — Make clear why this combination is better than any single discipline alone
6. **Maintain Rigor** — Disciplinary standards apply to each perspective; integration adds, not subtracts

Your interdisciplinary work is a strength. Make it intentional, transparent, and rigorous.
