# Reading Plan

## Purpose
Build a focused, time-bound reading plan that aligns with the current dissertation phase — preventing both under-reading (thin literature base) and over-reading (endless literature searching instead of writing).

## The Reading Trap
Many doctoral learners over-read as a form of procrastination. More sources feel like progress. But past a threshold (typically 80–120 sources for most dissertations), additional reading yields diminishing returns. The plan must be bounded.

**The rule:** Read to answer specific questions. Stop when the questions are answered.

## Reading Phases and Goals

| Dissertation Phase | Reading Goal | Approximate Sources |
|-------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Foundations** | Familiarize with the field; identify the gap | 30–60 sources |
| **Proposal writing** | Cover all Chapter 2 themes + theoretical framework | 60–100 sources |
| **Data collection** | Minimal — stay current, note new relevant work | 5–15 sources |
| **Analysis** | Revisit key sources that inform analysis approach | 10–20 sources |
| **Writing Chapters 4–5** | Fill gaps as findings demand comparison to literature | 10–20 new sources |

## Process

### Step 1 — Gather Inputs
Ask the learner:
1. What phase are you in?
2. What is your topic and research questions?
3. How many sources do you already have?
4. What themes or areas feel thin or under-sourced?
5. How much time per week can you dedicate to reading?

### Step 2 — Identify Reading Priorities
Based on the phase and gaps, identify:
- **Must-read category:** Sources directly addressing the core phenomenon
- **Should-read category:** Sources addressing theoretical framework and methodology
- **Nice-to-read category:** Adjacent or tangential sources

Assign time proportionally: 60% must-read, 30% should-read, 10% nice-to-read.

### Step 3 — Build the Search Strategy

**Database priorities:**
- ProQuest Dissertations and Theses
- EBSCOhost (Academic Search Complete, ERIC, Business Source Complete)
- PsycINFO
- Google Scholar (for citation chasing)
- Sage Journals, Taylor & Francis, Wiley (for specific journals)

**Search terms to develop:**
- Core phenomenon terms (with synonyms and related terms)
- Population/context terms
- Methodology terms
- Theoretical framework terms

**Boolean logic reminders:**
- AND — narrows results (servant leadership AND higher education)
- OR — broadens results (faculty OR professor OR academic staff)
- NOT — excludes terms
- Quotation marks — exact phrase ("transformational leadership")

### Step 4 — Set the Reading Schedule

**Reading session structure (60–90 minutes):**
1. Open 5–10 abstracts; screen for relevance (10 min)
2. Skim selected articles: intro + headings + conclusion (20–30 min)
3. Deep-read 1–2 highly relevant sources (30–40 min)
4. Process notes into second brain (10 min)

**Weekly reading targets by phase:**
- Foundations: 5–8 sources per week
- Proposal: 3–5 sources per week
- Analysis/Writing: 1–3 sources per week (as needed)

### Step 5 — Build a Reading List

Return a structured list:
- Category (must/should/nice)
- Search terms to use
- Databases to search
- Target number of sources per category
- Weekly reading time needed

## Reading Processing Rules
- Always take notes in a retrievable format (see `note-to-insight`)
- Log every source in the source log (see `source-log`)
- Ask for each source: "How does this connect to my research questions?"
- Stop searching when you reach saturation: finding the same authors/studies repeatedly

## Output Format
1. **Phase assessment** — where the learner is and what they need
2. **Priority reading categories** (must/should/nice with targets)
3. **Search strategy** (terms, databases, Boolean logic)
4. **Weekly reading schedule** (sessions per week, sources per session)
5. **Stop condition** — when to stop reading and start writing
6. **Next step** — what to do with the sources once gathered (source-synthesis, literature-review)
