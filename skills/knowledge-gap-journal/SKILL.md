# Knowledge Gap Journal

## Purpose
Capture, organize, and prioritize the learner's open questions and knowledge gaps — turning "I don't know" moments into a structured inquiry list that drives purposeful reading and prevents aimless searching.

## The Problem This Solves
When a learner encounters something they don't understand, the natural response is either:
1. Stop and search immediately (breaking flow)
2. Try to ignore it (and forget it)
3. Feel anxious about not knowing

The knowledge gap journal provides a third option:
4. **Capture it, classify it, and return to it deliberately** — converting unknowns into an organized learning backlog

## Journal Entry Format

Each entry has four fields:
```
QUESTION: [The specific thing I don't know or can't answer]
TYPE: [Type of gap — see below]
PRIORITY: [High / Medium / Low]
CONTEXT: [Where this came from — which article, chapter, or conversation surfaced it]
STATUS: [Open / In Progress / Resolved]
RESOLVED BY: [Source or explanation that answered it, when resolved]
```

## Types of Knowledge Gaps

**Conceptual:** I don't understand what this term or concept means
> "What is the difference between credibility and transferability in qualitative research?"

**Theoretical:** I don't understand how this theory works or applies
> "How does social exchange theory explain the leader-follower dynamic specifically?"

**Methodological:** I don't know how to execute this part of the research
> "How do I determine sample size adequacy for a phenomenological study?"

**Literature:** I haven't read enough on this topic to speak to it
> "What does the literature say about servant leadership in community colleges?"

**Argumentative:** I can't yet make this claim because I lack evidence
> "I claim that X, but I don't have sources to support it"

**Statistical/Analytical:** I don't know how to analyze this type of data
> "How do I run a hierarchical multiple regression in SPSS?"

## Process

### Step 1 — Capture
Any time the learner encounters something they don't know, they should:
1. Write it down immediately in one sentence
2. Classify it by type
3. Assign a priority (does this block the dissertation?)
4. Note where it came from

**High priority:** Blocks writing or committee approval
**Medium priority:** Important to understand soon but not blocking
**Low priority:** Interesting; resolve when time permits

### Step 2 — Weekly Review
Once per week (integrate with `weekly-review`):
1. Review all open items
2. Resolve any that can be answered quickly (a definition, a formula, a simple search)
3. Convert high-priority gaps into reading plan items (see `reading-plan`)
4. Archive resolved items

### Step 3 — Use Gaps to Drive Reading
The knowledge gap journal should directly inform:
- What to search for next in the database
- Which sources to read deeply vs. skim
- Which questions to ask the committee chair
- Which methodology texts to consult

### Step 4 — Close Gaps Deliberately
When a gap is resolved:
1. Write a 2–3 sentence summary of the answer
2. Note the source
3. Link to any notes, drafts, or dissertation sections it affects
4. Mark as resolved with date

## Sample Entries

```
QUESTION: What is the distinction between a case study and an ethnography?
TYPE: Methodological
PRIORITY: High
CONTEXT: Reviewing Chapter 3 methodology — committee may ask
STATUS: Open
```

```
QUESTION: What survey instruments measure servant leadership?
TYPE: Literature
PRIORITY: High
CONTEXT: Need for Chapter 3 instrumentation section
STATUS: Resolved
RESOLVED BY: Liden et al. (2008) — Servant Leadership Survey (SLS); van Dierendonck & Nuijten (2011) — Servant Leadership Survey
```

## Weekly Gap Review Prompt
> "What am I currently assuming is true that I haven't verified?"
> "What question came up this week that I wrote off instead of investigating?"
> "What is the single most important gap to close before my next writing session?"

## Output Format
1. **New entries formatted** (based on what the learner shares)
2. **Classified and prioritized** (each entry typed and ranked)
3. **Resolved vs. open count**
4. **Top 3 high-priority gaps to address this week**
5. **Reading plan connections** (which gaps should feed directly into reading sessions)
6. **Questions to bring to the chair** (gaps that require expert guidance)
