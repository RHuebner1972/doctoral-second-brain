# Weekly Review

## Purpose
Guide the learner through a structured weekly review of doctoral progress — celebrating wins, diagnosing slippage, recalibrating the plan, and committing to next week's priorities.

## Why Weekly Reviews Matter
The dissertation is a multi-year project with no external deadlines until committee checkpoints. Without a weekly review practice, months can pass in the illusion of busyness without meaningful progress. The weekly review creates a forcing function: face the reality of the week and make a conscious decision about next week.

## The Review Structure (5 Parts, 20–30 minutes)

---

### Part 1 — Capture and Close (5 min)
*What happened this week that's worth noting?*

- What did I complete? (anything, even small)
- What reading or sources did I engage with?
- What ideas came up that I haven't recorded yet?
- What commitments did I make to myself or others?

**Prompt:** "If I were telling a colleague what I got done this week on my dissertation, I would say..."

---

### Part 2 — Reality Check (5 min)
*What did I plan to do vs. what actually happened?*

- What did I commit to last week that I did not do?
- What got in the way?
- Was the interference unavoidable (life) or avoidable (avoidance)?
- Am I trending ahead of, on, or behind my timeline?

**The Honest Assessment:**
- [ ] On track
- [ ] Slightly behind (recoverable with one good week)
- [ ] Significantly behind (need to recalibrate the plan)
- [ ] In a genuine stall (need to diagnose the real problem)

---

### Part 3 — Learning and Insight (5 min)
*What did I learn this week — about my topic or about how I work?*

- What did I read, write, or think about that shifted my understanding?
- What new questions emerged?
- What writing or research approach worked or didn't?
- What should I add to my second brain (notes, concept map, source log)?

---

### Part 4 — Plan Next Week (10 min)
*What are my top 3 commitments for next week?*

Constraints:
- Maximum 3 dissertation commitments (overcommitting guarantees failure)
- At least 1 must be a writing or drafting task (not just reading)
- Each must have a measurable done condition

**Commitment format:**
> "By [day], I will have [specific output]."
> Example: "By Thursday, I will have a complete rough draft of the data analysis subsection of Chapter 3."

Also plan:
- How many writing sessions will I schedule?
- What day/time are they? (Put them in the calendar NOW)
- What would make next week a success?

---

### Part 5 — Mindset Close (2 min)
*End with intention, not guilt.*

- What am I most proud of from this week?
- What is the one thing I will NOT let slide next week?
- What is one reason this work matters?

---

## Patterns to Monitor Over Time

If the learner is doing these reviews consistently, watch for:

**Repeated slippage on the same task:** → Investigate avoidance (invoke `procrastination-audit`)

**Consistently overcommitting:** → Cut commitments to 2 instead of 3; build in recovery time

**No writing sessions completed for 2+ weeks:** → Escalate to `motivation-reset`

**Making progress but feeling behind:** → Check against the `dissertation-timeline`; the feeling may be inaccurate

## Output Format
1. **Capture list** — everything the learner accomplished (formatted as wins)
2. **Reality check assessment** — honest status (on track / behind / stalled)
3. **Slippage diagnosis** — what didn't happen and brief root cause
4. **Three commitments for next week** (with done conditions)
5. **Calendar blocking prompt** — "When this week will you write?" (specific times)
6. **Closing statement** — one sentence of acknowledgment and forward intention
