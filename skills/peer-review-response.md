# Peer Review Response Builder

## Overview

Responding to committee or peer reviewer feedback is a skill in itself. A strong response is professional, specific, grateful in tone, and demonstrates that you engaged seriously with every comment — whether you agreed with it or not. This skill helps you draft point-by-point response letters and revision notes that build committee confidence and move your work forward.

---

## Intake Questions

1. **Paste the feedback you received.** (Include each comment, numbered or labeled.)
2. **What is the source of the feedback?** (dissertation chair, committee member, journal peer reviewer, proposal defense)
3. **For each comment: do you agree, partially agree, or disagree?** (If unsure, flag it.)
4. **What revisions have you made (or plan to make) in response?**

---

## Response Philosophy

**Three rules for responding to reviewers:**

1. **Every comment gets a response** — even if the answer is "No change was made because…"
2. **Tone is always collegial and appreciative** — reviewers are doing you a favor even when their comments sting
3. **Be specific** — say exactly what you changed and where (page number, paragraph, line)

**Never:**
- Argue defensively or dismiss feedback without explanation
- Make changes without explaining them in the response letter
- Ignore a comment because you don't like it

## Response Letter Structure

```
Dear [Chair name / Committee / Reviewers],

Thank you for your thoughtful feedback on [dissertation chapter / manuscript title].
Your comments have strengthened the work considerably. I have carefully reviewed
each comment and responded below. Revisions are noted with page and paragraph
references for ease of review.

---

REVIEWER [1] / COMMITTEE MEMBER [Name]

Comment 1: [Paste original comment]

Response: [Your response — see templates below]

Revision made: [What you changed and where — "Revised paragraph 2 on page 14 to…"]

---

Comment 2: [Continue for each comment]

---

Thank you again for your investment in this work. I believe these revisions have
substantially improved the manuscript and I look forward to your continued feedback.

Respectfully,
[Your name]
```

## Response Templates by Scenario

### When You Agree and Made the Change
```
Thank you for this feedback. The reviewer is correct that [restate the issue].
In response, I have [describe exactly what you did — added a citation, clarified a definition,
restructured a paragraph, etc.]. This revision appears on page [X], paragraph [Y].
The updated text now reads: "[Brief quote of the revised text if helpful]."
```

### When You Partially Agree
```
I appreciate this observation. I agree that [aspect you accept] and have revised
accordingly [describe revision, page/paragraph]. However, I respectfully maintain that
[aspect you're keeping], because [scholarly rationale with citation if possible].
To clarify this distinction, I have added [explanation] on page [X].
```

### When You Disagree (Handled Diplomatically)
```
I appreciate the reviewer's concern about [issue]. After careful consideration,
I respectfully maintain the original approach for the following reasons:
[Reason 1 — with scholarly support or methodological justification].
[Reason 2 if applicable].
However, to address the reviewer's concern about potential confusion, I have added
a clarifying sentence on page [X] that [what it does].
```

### When a Comment Is About Clarity, Not Content
```
Thank you for flagging this unclear passage. I have revised the sentence/paragraph
on page [X] for clarity. The original text read: "[original]." The revised text reads:
"[revision]."
```

### When You Need More Information
```
I want to ensure I fully address this comment. Could you clarify whether your concern is
[interpretation A] or [interpretation B]? I am prepared to [revision option A or B]
once I understand the intent of the comment.
```

## Committee Feedback Specific Notes

**Dissertation Chair vs. Committee Member:**
- Chair feedback carries more weight and typically reflects program standards
- Committee member feedback may occasionally conflict with the chair — flag this to your chair before revising
- If two committee members disagree, ask your chair to adjudicate

**Common Committee Comments and How to Address Them:**

| Comment Type | What They Really Mean | How to Respond |
|---|---|---|
| "Your problem statement is unclear." | The gap, evidence, and study positioning aren't distinct. | Use the `problem-statement` skill to rebuild it with the four-move architecture. |
| "The literature review is descriptive, not synthetic." | You're summarizing sources instead of synthesizing them. | Use `source-synthesis` to identify themes; rewrite with synthesis language. |
| "How does your framework connect to your RQs?" | The framework feels bolted on, not integrated. | Add explicit connections between framework components and each RQ. |
| "Justify your sample size." | You haven't cited a methodological rationale for your N. | Add a power analysis citation (quant) or saturation rationale (qual). |
| "Be more specific in your implications." | "Future research should study this" is too vague. | Name the population, context, and specific variable/phenomenon for each recommendation. |

## Next Steps

2. Make all revisions in the manuscript first, noting page/paragraph numbers
3. Draft the response letter using the templates above
4. Have your chair review the response letter before submitting to the full committee
