# Committee & Advisor Relationship Management

## Purpose
Establish and maintain productive relationships with advisors and committee members that support dissertation progress, facilitate feedback cycles, and resolve conflicts constructively.

## Process

### Step 1 — Select Your Committee Strategically

**Committee Composition** (typically 3–5 members: chair + 2–3 additional members)

**Your Chair (Primary Advisor)**
- Traditionally holds highest degree in your field
- Must approve all major decisions (methods, timeline, revisions)
- Your primary point of contact for feedback
- Selection criteria:
  - Expertise aligned with your dissertation topic
  - Research productivity (active scholar, publishes)
  - Known mentorship style (supportive, responsive, available)
  - Realistic availability (not overcommitted)
  - Aligned with institutional/program requirements

**Additional Committee Members**
- **Content Expert**: Deep expertise in your specific research area
- **Methodologist**: Expert in your research approach (qualitative, quantitative, mixed methods)
- **Program Requirement**: Often a statistician (quant) or qualitative methods specialist
- Optional: **External Member** from outside your institution (adds credibility)

**Selection Strategy**
- Ask potential members informally before nominating officially
- Frame it positively: "I'm impressed by your work in [X]; would you consider joining my committee?"
- Ensure diversity of perspective (avoid all agreeing with each other)
- Confirm complementary expertise (don't duplicate skills)
- Verify availability before formal nomination

**Avoid Red Flags**
🚩 Chair who is rarely available or unresponsive  
🚩 Committee members with conflicting research philosophies who cannot resolve differences  
🚩 Overcommitted faculty who take months to review chapters  
🚩 Committee incompatible with your topic (e.g., wrong methodological expertise)

### Step 2 — Set Up for Success: The Kick-Off Meeting

**Before the Meeting**
- Email committee members your dissertation prospectus or proposal (if available)
- Suggest 2–3 meeting times; ask for availability
- Prepare a brief agenda (5–7 minutes)

**Kick-Off Meeting Agenda**
1. **Introduction** (1 min)
   - Thank them for serving; express genuine appreciation
2. **Dissertation Snapshot** (3 mins)
   - Brief overview of problem, RQs, methods
   - Why you're passionate about this work
3. **Timeline & Expectations** (2 mins)
   - Expected graduation timeline
   - How often you'll submit chapters
   - Preferred feedback format (email, in-person, meetings, comments on draft?)
4. **Roles & Responsibilities** (2 mins)
   - Make explicit: Chair provides primary feedback; committee provides secondary review
   - Clarify response time expectations (e.g., "I aim to provide feedback within 2 weeks")
5. **Questions & Open Discussion** (3–5 mins)
   - Ask if they have expectations for you
   - Clarify any uncertainties

**Document Agreed Expectations**
Send a follow-up email: "Here's what I understood from our meeting: [summarize]. Please let me know if this matches your expectations."

### Step 3 — Manage Your Relationship with Your Chair

**Best Practices with Your Advisor**

**Communication Cadence**
- Monthly check-ins (in-person or video call; 30 mins)
- Interim email updates if issues arise
- Pre-plan meeting agendas to respect their time
- Respond promptly to their feedback

**Sample Meeting Template**
1. **What I've completed since last meeting** (5 mins)
2. **What I'm working on now** (5 mins)
3. **Obstacles or questions** (10 mins)
4. **Next steps & timeline** (5 mins)
5. **Any feedback on recent chapter draft** (5 mins)

**Submitting Drafts**
- Check your advisor's preferences: do they like early rough drafts for feedback, or polished drafts?
- Always include a submission email noting:
  - What you're asking feedback on specifically (whole chapter, methods only, argument flow, etc.)
  - Where you're stuck or uncertain
  - Expected turnaround you need
- Example: "Here's my Methods chapter. I'm most uncertain about my sampling justification—would appreciate specific feedback there. I need your review by [date] to meet my revision timeline."

**If Your Advisor Is Unresponsive**
- Follow up after 2 weeks with a gentle email reminder
- If no response after another week, ask for a brief meeting to discuss timelines
- If persistent unresponsiveness: escalate to department chair or graduate director
- Consider this a red flag for graduation timeline; plan accordingly

**Managing Style Differences**
- Some advisors prefer independence; others want regular check-ins
- Some give detailed feedback; others high-level guidance
- Ask explicitly: "I want to work well together. What's your preferred cadence and feedback style?"

### Step 4 — Navigate Conflicting Committee Feedback

**Common Scenario**
- Chair wants you to emphasize practical implications
- Methodologist criticizes your sample size
- Content expert suggests a different theoretical lens

**Your Role in Conflict**
1. **Document all feedback** (verbatim or summarized)
2. **Identify overlap vs. divergence**
   - Where do committee members agree?
   - Where is legitimate disagreement?
3. **Prioritize your chair's guidance** (chair has final authority unless otherwise specified)
4. **Synthesize thoughtfully** (you don't have to accept every suggestion, but you must address each one)

**Response Strategy**
Schedule a **consensus meeting** with your chair + conflicting committee member:
- "I received feedback suggesting [A] and [B]. I want to understand your perspectives and chart a path forward."
- Let them discuss; your chair typically mediates
- Ask: "What's the minimum viable version of [X] that addresses both concerns?"
- Get clarity in writing: "So my revised approach will be [describe]; does that align?"

**When Committee Feedback Contradicts**
- If both are correct (e.g., two valid methodological approaches):
  - Choose one; document why in your methods chapter
  - Acknowledge the alternative in your limitations
- If one is objectively wrong:
  - Respectfully push back: "I appreciate that perspective. Here's why I'm proceeding differently: [reason]."
  - In writing (email) so there's a record

**Documenting Conflicts**
- Keep all feedback in a shared location (Google Drive folder with advisor; makes decisions transparent)
- Email after meetings: "Here's my understanding of feedback received: [summary]. Proceeding with [decision]."
- Your chair should approve major changes to direction

### Step 5 — Managing Revisions After Feedback

**Revision Response Matrix** (track & respond to every comment)

| Feedback | Your Response | Decision |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| "Results section is unclear" | Clarify findings; add visualization | Incorporated |
| "Consider Foucault's theory" | Read Foucault; assess fit with my framework | Considered; determined not aligned; brief explanation in text |
| "Sample size too small" | Justify sample size theoretically & practically | Kept sample; added sensitivity analysis |

**Revision Communication**
When you resubmit revised chapters:
- Create a revision summary: "Here are the changes I made in response to feedback: [list each change & what you changed]"
- Highlight revised sections (track changes or color-coded)
- Include a note if you chose NOT to incorporate feedback: "You suggested [X]; I considered this and decided [Y] because [reason]"
- Ask: "Does this address your concerns?"

### Step 6 — Handle Difficult Committee Dynamics

**Scenario: Committee Member Is Critical/Harsh**
- Separate the criticism from the person: "What is the valid feedback here, regardless of tone?"
- Discuss with chair if behavior is unprofessional
- Remember: This person is expert in their domain; lean into their expertise even if delivery is rough

**Scenario: Committee Member Holds You to Unrealistic Standards**
- Chair mediates: "Is this feedback essential for graduation?"
- Set boundaries: "I appreciate the suggestion. Given my timeline, here's what's realistic."
- Document in email: "I'm interpreting your feedback as: [X]. I'm implementing [Y] to address this."

**Scenario: Advisor & Committee Disagree on Your Readiness**
- Chair has institutional authority
- If chair says you're ready for defense, you move forward
- If committee member objects: "I have concerns about [X]." Chair responds.
- Trust the process; this is rare

### Step 7 — Communication Templates

**Email to Potential Committee Member**
```
Subject: Invitation to Serve on Dissertation Committee

Dear [Name],

I am writing to ask if you would consider serving on my dissertation committee. Your expertise in [specific area] directly aligns with my research on [topic]. I deeply respect your work in [specific contribution], and I believe your perspective would significantly strengthen my work.

My dissertation focuses on [brief description]. I anticipate graduation in [semester/year].

Would you be willing to serve in this role? I would be happy to discuss my research and answer any questions you may have.

Thank you for considering this invitation.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

**Email Submitting Draft Chapter**
```
Subject: [Chapter Name] Draft for Review

Dear [Advisor Name],

Attached is my draft [chapter name]. I would appreciate your feedback on:
1. The coherence of my [methodology/argument/analysis]
2. Whether my [specific element] adequately addresses my [RQ/framework]

I am particularly uncertain about [X]; any guidance there would be invaluable.

I need your review by [date] to stay on track for [next milestone]. Please let me know if you need more time.

Thank you,
[Your name]
```

**Revision Response Email**
```
Subject: Revision Response – [Chapter Name]

Dear [Advisor Name],

I have completed revisions to [chapter name] based on your feedback. Below is a summary of changes:

1. Clarified Methods section (p. 3–4): [describe change]
2. Added results visualization (new Fig. 2): [describe]
3. Expanded Discussion of [X] (p. 10–12): [describe]

Regarding your suggestion about [feedback not incorporated]: I considered this carefully. Here's why I proceeded differently: [brief reasoning].

I've attached the revised chapter with track changes. Please review and let me know if these revisions address your concerns.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

## Committee Management Checklist

- [ ] Identify potential chair based on expertise, availability, mentorship style
- [ ] Identify 2–3 additional committee members (content expert, methodologist)
- [ ] Have informal conversations; confirm willingness before formal nomination
- [ ] Schedule kick-off meeting once committee is official
- [ ] Set explicit expectations for communication cadence & feedback format
- [ ] Establish regular check-ins with advisor (monthly or as needed)
- [ ] Document all feedback systematically
- [ ] Identify overlapping vs. conflicting guidance
- [ ] Prioritize chair's guidance in case of conflict
- [ ] Respond to every piece of feedback (incorporated or explained)
- [ ] Submit revised work with clear revision tracking & summary
- [ ] Escalate conflicts to chair/department if necessary
- [ ] Maintain professional, appreciative tone in all communications

---

## Red Flags in Committee Relationships

🚩 Advisor unresponsive for 3+ weeks  
🚩 Committee members giving fundamentally incompatible guidance with no path to resolution  
🚩 Advisor discouraging your research without explanation  
🚩 Committee member repeatedly asking for major rewrites after approving direction  
🚩 Feeling pressured or micromanaged without clear learning value  

**Action**: Escalate to your graduate program director or department chair if concerning patterns emerge.
