# Motivation Reset

## Purpose
Help the learner reconnect with their purpose, reclaim their sense of progress, and exit a motivational low — not by pumping them up, but by helping them see clearly.

## What This Is NOT
This is not a pep talk. Pep talks wear off in an hour.

This is a structured reset — a process that changes what the learner is attending to, so that their intrinsic motivation can re-engage.

## The Three Sources of Doctoral Motivation Loss

**1. Disconnection from purpose**
The learner has lost sight of *why* this matters. The dissertation has become a bureaucratic obstacle rather than a meaningful contribution. Fix: reconnect to the original "why."

**2. Distorted progress perception**
The learner is making progress but can't see it — because they're comparing themselves to an imagined version of where they should be. Fix: create an accurate progress inventory.

**3. Genuine depletion / burnout**
The learner is actually exhausted — cognitively, emotionally, or physically. Pushing through is counterproductive. Fix: prescribe recovery before work.

## The Reset Process

### Phase 1 — Check In (No Judgment)
Start with compassion, not critique. Ask:
1. "How long have you been feeling this way?"
2. "What specifically feels hard right now — the writing itself, the topic, the process, or something outside the dissertation?"
3. "When did you last feel good about your work?"

**Diagnose the type:**
- If purpose-related → go to Phase 2
- If progress-related → go to Phase 3
- If depletion-related → go to Phase 4

---

### Phase 2 — Purpose Reconnection

Ask:
1. "Why did you start this doctoral program? What was the original reason?"
2. "Who will benefit if you finish this work?"
3. "What problem in the world does your research address?"
4. "What does 'Dr.' in front of your name mean to you? To your family? To your community?"
5. "If you quit today, what would you be walking away from?"

Then: Help the learner write a 3–5 sentence **purpose statement** — not an academic one. A personal one.
> "I am doing this because..."

Pin this somewhere visible. Return to it when the tank runs low.

---

### Phase 3 — Progress Inventory

The learner doesn't see what they've done. Build a complete list:

1. Courses completed
2. Sources read (approximate)
3. Pages or words written
4. Committee formed / relationships built
5. Concepts mastered that they couldn't have explained a year ago
6. Milestones passed (proposal, IRB, data collection, etc.)

Then: Compare to where they started. The progress is real. They are not the same person who enrolled.

> "Here is what you have actually done..."

---

### Phase 4 — Depletion Protocol

When the learner is genuinely burned out:
1. **Stop.** Prescribe a short, defined break — 3 days to 2 weeks.
2. **Don't abandon** — make a specific return date. Write it down.
3. **During the break:** No reading, no thinking about the dissertation. Full recovery.
4. **Before returning:** Use the `writing-session-plan` skill for a gentle re-entry — 15 minutes only.

**Signs of burnout vs. normal avoidance:**
| Avoidance | Burnout |
|-----------|---------|
| "I don't want to" | "I physically can't" |
| Procrastinating on specific sections | Avoiding the dissertation entirely for weeks |
| Guilt | Numbness or resentment |
| Responds to a structured plan | Doesn't respond to plans |

---

### Phase 5 — The Smallest Credible Next Action

Regardless of which path was taken, end with one specific, tiny action:
> "The one thing I will do in the next 24 hours is: ___"

It must be so small it's undeniable. Examples:
- "Open the document and read the last paragraph I wrote"
- "Write one sentence"
- "Send my chair a one-paragraph update"
- "Sit down for 10 minutes with the dissertation, even if I write nothing"

## What NOT to Do
- Do NOT prescribe a huge to-do list after a motivation reset
- Do NOT compare the learner to others
- Do NOT minimize how hard this is
- Do NOT tell them "just push through"

## Output Format
1. **Type of motivation loss** (purpose / progress / depletion)
2. **Phase-specific reset** (purpose reconnection, progress inventory, or depletion protocol)
3. **Personal purpose statement** (drafted with the learner)
4. **Progress inventory** (if applicable — a genuine list of accomplishments)
5. **Smallest credible next action** (one thing, doable today or tomorrow)
6. **Return plan** (if break was prescribed — when and how to come back)
