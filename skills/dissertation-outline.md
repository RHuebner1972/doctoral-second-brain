# Dissertation Outline & Chapter Guide

## Overview

Most dissertations — regardless of discipline — follow a five-chapter model. This skill provides a full scaffold for that model, with section-level guidance for each chapter, typical word counts, and the key questions each section must answer. Use this as your structural map from proposal through final defense.

---

## Intake Questions

1. **What is your methodology?** (qualitative, quantitative, mixed methods)
2. **What is your degree type?** (PhD, EdD, DBA — affects emphasis on theory vs. practice)
3. **What is your institution / program?** (Some programs have specific templates — flag this if you have one)
4. **What stage are you at?** (outlining, drafting, revising)

---

## The Five-Chapter Model

```
Chapter 1: Introduction
Chapter 2: Literature Review
Chapter 3: Methodology
Chapter 4: Results / Findings
Chapter 5: Discussion, Conclusions, and Recommendations
```

*Note: Some programs use a three-paper (manuscript) model or other alternative formats. The five-chapter model is the most common and is described here.*

---

## Chapter 1: Introduction

**Purpose:** Establish the context, problem, purpose, significance, and design of the study. Orient the reader and justify the entire dissertation.

**Typical length:** 15–25 pages / 3,500–6,000 words

| Section | What It Does | Key Questions to Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **Background of the Study** | Establishes the broad context and why the topic matters | What is the domain? Why does it matter now? Who cares? |
| **Problem Statement** | Identifies the specific, documented problem | What is the problem? Who is affected? What evidence shows it exists? |
| **Purpose Statement** | States what the study will do | What is the study's goal? What will it produce? |
| **Research Questions** | Presents the central and sub-questions | What exactly will the study answer? |
| **Theoretical / Conceptual Framework** | Names the framework and why it fits | What theory grounds this study? How does it connect to the RQs? |
| **Nature of the Study** | Summarizes the design approach | Why qualitative/quantitative/mixed? What is the design? |
| **Significance of the Study** | Makes the "so what" case | Why does this matter for theory? For practice? For policy? |
| **Scope and Delimitations** | Defines what the study covers (and intentionally excludes) | What is in scope? What is not, and why? |
| **Limitations** | Acknowledges potential weaknesses | What could threaten validity/credibility? What can't you control? |
| **Assumptions** | States what you take as true without testing | What assumptions underlie your design? |
| **Definition of Terms** | Operationalizes key constructs | How are key terms defined for this study? |
| **Summary / Transition** | Closes Chapter 1 and previews Chapter 2 | What does Chapter 2 cover? |

**Purpose Statement Template:**
```
The purpose of this [qualitative/quantitative/mixed methods] [design name] study
is to [explore/examine/describe/determine] [the phenomenon/relationship/experience]
of [population] in [context/setting]. At this stage of the research, [construct]
will be generally defined as [working definition].
```

## Chapter 2: Literature Review

**Purpose:** Demonstrate mastery of the scholarly conversation. Establish the theoretical framework. Build the case for your gap. Show that your study is needed and positioned correctly.

**Typical length:** 25–40 pages / 6,000–10,000 words

| Section | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Preview the themes and structure of the review |
| **Theoretical / Conceptual Framework** | Full treatment of the framework — origins, components, empirical support, fit |
| **Theme 1** | Major construct 1 — origins, definitions, empirical findings, debates |
| **Theme 2** | Major construct 2 — same structure |
| **Theme 3** | Intersection or relationship between constructs |
| **Summary & Gap** | Synthesize themes, articulate the gap, bridge to Chapter 3 |

Use the `literature-review` skill for detailed guidance on structuring and writing this chapter.

## Chapter 3: Methodology

**Purpose:** Explain and justify every design decision. Enable replication. Demonstrate methodological rigor.

**Typical length:** 15–25 pages / 4,000–6,000 words

| Section | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Preview Chapter 3; restate purpose and RQs |
| **Research Design and Rationale** | Name the design; justify it; cite methodologists; reject alternatives |
| **Role of the Researcher** | (Qualitative) Reflexivity, bracketing, positionality |
| **Participants / Population** | Who will be studied; inclusion/exclusion criteria |
| **Sampling Strategy** | How participants will be selected (purposive, random, stratified, etc.) |
| **Sample Size Rationale** | Why this number is appropriate (saturation, power analysis, etc.) |
| **Data Collection Instruments** | What tools will be used (interview protocol, survey, observation guide) |
| **Data Collection Procedures** | How data will actually be collected, step by step |
| **Data Analysis Plan** | How data will be analyzed (coding, thematic analysis, statistical tests) |
| **Trustworthiness** (qual) / **Validity & Reliability** (quant) | How rigor is established |
| **Ethical Considerations** | Informed consent, IRB, confidentiality, risks |
| **Summary** | Recap design; preview Chapter 4 |

Use the `methodology` skill for design selection and justification language.

## Chapter 4: Results / Findings

**Purpose:** Present what you found, organized by research question. Do not interpret — that's Chapter 5.

**Typical length:** 20–40 pages (varies widely by method and amount of data)

| Section | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Restate purpose and RQs; preview how the chapter is organized |
| **Participant / Sample Description** | Describe who participated (demographics, characteristics) |
| **Research Question 1** (and findings) | Present data/findings organized by RQ1 |
| **Research Question 2** (and findings) | Repeat for each RQ |
| **Summary** | Synthesize key findings; preview Chapter 5 |

**Qualitative:** Present themes with supporting quotes; label themes with descriptive names.
**Quantitative:** Present descriptive statistics → inferential statistics → test results for each hypothesis.

---

## Chapter 5: Discussion, Conclusions, and Recommendations

**Purpose:** Interpret findings, connect to the literature, state conclusions, acknowledge limitations, and recommend future directions.

**Typical length:** 15–25 pages / 4,000–7,000 words

| Section | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Restate purpose, questions, and brief overview of what was found |
| **Summary of Findings** | High-level summary — not a repeat of Chapter 4 in detail |
| **Discussion of Findings** | Interpret each finding; connect to literature, theory, and gap |
| **Theoretical Implications** | What does this mean for the framework/theory? |
| **Practical Implications** | What should practitioners do with this? |
| **Policy Implications** | (if applicable) What should change at the policy level? |
| **Limitations** | Revisit limitations; be honest about what constrains generalizability |
| **Recommendations for Future Research** | What should the next study do? What questions remain? |
| **Conclusions** | Final synthesis — what do you now know that you didn't know before? |
| **Closing Statement** | One paragraph — why this work matters |

## Front and Back Matter

**Front Matter:**
- Title Page
- Signature/Approval Page
- Abstract (350–500 words)
- Dedication (optional)
- Acknowledgments
- Table of Contents
- List of Tables
- List of Figures

**Back Matter:**
- References (APA 7th edition)
- Appendices (consent forms, instruments, IRB approval, codebooks, etc.)
