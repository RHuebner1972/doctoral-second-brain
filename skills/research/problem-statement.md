# Problem Statement Builder

## Overview

The problem statement is the cornerstone of Chapter 1. It answers the question: *Why does this study need to exist?* A strong problem statement (1) establishes that a problem exists in the real world, (2) grounds that claim in evidence, (3) connects it to the research gap in the literature, and (4) positions the proposed study as the logical response. This skill walks you through the construction of that statement and produces a polished, citable draft.

---

## Intake Questions

Ask the learner these questions before drafting. Do not guess at missing information.

1. **What is the problem you want to study?** Describe it in plain language first.
2. **Who is affected by this problem, and how?** (individuals, organizations, communities)
3. **What evidence shows this problem exists?** (statistics, reports, policy documents, practitioner accounts)
4. **What does the existing literature say about this problem?** What's been tried or studied?
5. **What is the research gap that your study addresses?** (Use `research-gap` output if available)
6. **What is your degree type?** (PhD, EdD, DBA — affects whether the problem is theoretical, practical, or both)
7. **What discipline or program are you in?**

## The Problem Statement Architecture

A dissertation problem statement typically contains four moves, in this order:

### Move 1: Establish the Scope (Hook)
Open with the broader context — what is the general domain, why does it matter, and who cares? This orients the reader before narrowing. Cite 1–2 foundational or authoritative sources.

> *Leadership in higher education institutions has come under increasing scrutiny as declining enrollment, shifting workforce expectations, and post-pandemic organizational stress have strained administrative capacity (AAUP, 2023; Kezar et al., 2021).*

### Move 2: Narrow to the Specific Problem
Identify the specific, documented problem. Avoid vague statements like "there is a problem with leadership." Be concrete: who is affected, in what setting, with what consequence?

> *Community college faculty report disproportionately high attrition rates compared to their four-year counterparts, with 34% citing leadership quality as a primary reason for departure (CUPA-HR, 2022). Despite institutional efforts to address retention, turnover among full-time faculty at community colleges has increased 18% since 2019.*

### Move 3: Connect to the Literature Gap
Establish that the scholarly literature has not adequately addressed this problem. Reference what HAS been studied to frame what HASN'T.

> *While servant leadership has been studied extensively in corporate and military contexts (Brown & Mitchell, 2010; Greenleaf, 1977), its relationship to faculty retention in community colleges remains underexplored. Existing studies have relied primarily on quantitative instruments with four-year university populations, leaving qualitative accounts from community college faculty largely absent.*

### Move 4: Position the Study
State clearly what the proposed study will do and why it is the appropriate response to this problem. Avoid claiming your study will "solve" the problem — claim it will *contribute to understanding.*

> *It is not known how community college faculty perceive servant leadership behaviors in their supervisors or how those perceptions relate to their intention to stay. A qualitative study exploring faculty experiences is needed to generate practitioner-relevant insights and contribute to the emerging literature on servant leadership in community college contexts.*

## Draft Template

```
[Context sentence establishing the broad domain and why it matters — 1–2 sentences, 1–2 citations.]

[Specific problem: who is affected, in what setting, with what consequence — 2–3 sentences, 2–3 empirical citations or data points.]

[Literature gap: what has been studied vs. what has not — 2–3 sentences connecting to the gap analysis.]

[Study positioning: what is not known, what the study will do, and why it is warranted — 2–3 sentences.]
```

---

## Quality Criteria

A strong problem statement:

- [ ] Is grounded in empirical evidence (statistics, reports, prior studies) — not just assertions
- [ ] Names the affected population and setting specifically
- [ ] Connects to a genuine gap in the scholarly literature
- [ ] Is written in third person, past or present tense, in scholarly register
- [ ] Does not reveal the solution or findings
- [ ] Is 200–400 words (typical dissertation range; check your program's guidelines)
- [ ] Ends with an "it is not known" or "there is a need for" statement that logically generates the purpose statement

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| "There is a problem with leadership in organizations." | Name the specific organization type, the specific leadership behavior, and cite evidence. |
| "Researchers have ignored this topic." | Show what researchers HAVE studied, then explain what they haven't. |
| "This study will solve the retention crisis." | Replace with: "This study will contribute to understanding of…" |
| No citations in the problem statement | Every factual claim about the problem needs a citation. |
| Problem statement and purpose statement are the same | The problem explains *why* the study is needed; the purpose states *what* the study will do. |
