# Research Questions

## Purpose
Develop precisely worded, methodologically aligned research questions (and hypotheses where applicable) that are answerable, bounded, and directly traceable to the problem statement.

## The Alignment Rule
Research questions must form a **golden thread**:
> Problem Statement → Gap → Research Questions → Methodology → Data Collection → Analysis → Findings

If any link breaks, the whole study breaks.

## Types of Research Questions by Methodology

### Qualitative Questions
- Begin with: *What*, *How*, or *Why*
- Explore lived experience, meaning, process, or phenomenon
- Do NOT presuppose direction or outcome
- **Central question** is broad; **sub-questions** are focused aspects of it

**Templates:**
> "What is the lived experience of [population] in [context]?"
> "How do [participants] make sense of [phenomenon]?"
> "What factors influence [process] for [population] in [setting]?"

### Quantitative Questions
- Examine relationships, differences, or predictions between variables
- Variables must be measurable and operationally defined
- Often paired with null and alternative hypotheses

**Templates:**
> "To what extent does [independent variable] predict [dependent variable] among [population]?"
> "Is there a significant difference in [outcome] between [Group A] and [Group B]?"
> "What is the relationship between [variable 1] and [variable 2] controlling for [covariate]?"

**Hypothesis format:**
> H₀: There is no significant relationship between [X] and [Y].
> H₁: There is a significant positive relationship between [X] and [Y].

### Mixed Methods Questions
- Combine qualitative and quantitative strands
- Include a meta-inference question that addresses how the strands integrate

**Templates:**
> "In what ways does [quantitative finding] help explain [qualitative theme]?" (explanatory sequential)
> "How do the quantitative results and qualitative findings converge or diverge regarding [phenomenon]?"

## Process

### Step 1 — Confirm the Problem and Gap
Ask: What is your problem statement and identified gap? What methodology are you using?

### Step 2 — Draft the Central Research Question
- One overarching question that, if answered, addresses the gap
- Should not be answerable with yes/no
- Should not have "and" (that means it's two questions)

### Step 3 — Draft Sub-Questions (2–5 recommended)
Each sub-question:
- Addresses a distinct facet of the central question
- Maps to a specific data source, instrument, or analysis procedure
- Together, sub-questions fully address the central question

### Step 4 — Alignment Check
For each question, confirm:
- [ ] Does it trace to the gap in the problem statement?
- [ ] Can data be collected to answer it?
- [ ] Is the methodology capable of answering it?
- [ ] Is it free of embedded assumptions or value judgments?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon or undefined terms?

### Step 5 — Format for the Dissertation
Present as:
> **Central Research Question:** [question]
> **RQ1:** [sub-question 1]
> **RQ2:** [sub-question 2]
> **RQ3:** [sub-question 3]

For quantitative: also list H₀ and H₁ for each RQ.

## Common Errors to Flag
- Questions answerable with "yes" or "no"
- Questions that presuppose a finding ("Why does X cause Y?" — assumes causation)
- Questions too broad to be answered in one study
- Questions whose answer is already known
- Mismatch between qualitative questions and quantitative methodology

## Output Format
1. **Central research question** (with rationale)
2. **Sub-questions** (numbered, with methodology alignment note for each)
3. **Hypotheses** (if quantitative or mixed)
4. **Alignment check results** (pass/flag for each criterion)
5. **Next step** — methodology selection or IRB considerations
