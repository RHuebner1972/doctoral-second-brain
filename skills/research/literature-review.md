# Literature Review Planner & Writer

## Overview

The literature review (typically Chapter 2) demonstrates that you have mastered the scholarly conversation surrounding your topic — and that your study makes an original contribution to it. It is NOT an annotated bibliography or a string of summaries. It is a *synthesis* that identifies themes, tensions, gaps, and the conceptual architecture your study inhabits. This skill helps you plan the structure, organize your sources, and draft the review.

---

## Intake Questions

1. **What is your research topic / central phenomenon?**
2. **What are your 3–5 core themes or constructs?** (e.g., servant leadership, faculty retention, organizational commitment)
3. **What methodology are you using?** (affects what types of literature to include)
4. **How many sources do you currently have?** Approximately.
5. **What is your dissertation type?** (PhD — theory-forward; EdD/DBA — practice-forward)
6. **Do you have a theoretical or conceptual framework?** What is it?
7. **What organizational structure do you prefer, or should I recommend one?**

---

## Choosing Your Organizational Structure

### Option 1: Thematic (Most Common)
Organize the review around the major themes or constructs in your study, regardless of publication date. Ideal when your topic spans multiple bodies of literature.

**Structure:**
```
Introduction
Theme 1: [First major construct — e.g., Servant Leadership]
  - Origins and theoretical development
  - Empirical studies: what we know
  - Critiques, debates, and unresolved issues
Theme 2: [Second major construct — e.g., Faculty Retention]
  - Overview of the retention literature
  - Factors associated with retention
  - Gaps in the population/context
Theme 3: [Intersection or relationship — e.g., Leadership and Retention]
  - Studies that connect both constructs
  - What's missing: your gap
Summary and Synthesis
Transition to Chapter 3
```

### Option 2: Conceptual/Framework-Driven
Organized around the theoretical framework. Sections correspond to the components or propositions of the framework. Ideal for theory-testing dissertations.

### Option 3: Chronological
Traces the evolution of the literature over time. Useful when paradigm shifts or historical context are central to your argument. Less common for most dissertations.

### Option 4: Methodological
Organizes literature by research design (qualitative studies, quantitative studies, mixed methods). Useful in methodology-focused dissertations or when you're arguing that a particular method is needed.

## Literature Review Writing Protocol

### Step 1: Source Mapping
Before writing, map your sources to your themes in a table:

| Source | Theme 1 | Theme 2 | Theme 3 | Gap/Critique | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smith (2018) | ✓ | | | | Foundational theory |
| Jones & Lee (2021) | ✓ | ✓ | | ✓ | Methodological critique |

### Step 2: Theme Paragraphs
Each theme section follows this micro-structure:
1. **Topic sentence** — state the claim or synthesis point for this paragraph
2. **Evidence** — cite 2–4 sources that support or complicate the claim
3. **Analysis** — explain what the pattern means, not just what the sources say
4. **Transition** — connect to the next paragraph or sub-theme

**Example paragraph:**
> Servant leadership has been consistently linked to positive organizational outcomes, including increased employee engagement, trust in leadership, and organizational citizenship behaviors (Ehrhart, 2004; Liden et al., 2008; van Dierendonck, 2011). However, the majority of these studies have been conducted in corporate and for-profit settings using self-report instruments with managerial populations (Brown & Mitchell, 2010). This reliance on a single sector and a single measurement approach limits the generalizability of findings and raises questions about whether servant leadership manifests similarly in mission-driven institutions such as community colleges.

### Step 3: Synthesis Moves
Avoid stringing summaries together. Use these synthesis moves:

- **Convergence:** "Multiple studies have found that X leads to Y (Smith, 2018; Jones, 2020; Lee, 2022)."
- **Divergence:** "While Smith (2018) found X, Jones (2020) reported contradictory results, suggesting that context may moderate this relationship."
- **Building:** "Building on Smith's (2018) conceptual model, subsequent researchers have empirically tested the relationship between X and Y in applied settings."
- **Gap:** "Despite this body of work, no study has examined X in the context of Y."

### Step 4: Introduction and Conclusion
- **Introduction:** Tell the reader what the review covers, how it is organized, and what it will establish.
- **Conclusion (Synthesis):** Synthesize the themes, articulate the gap, and bridge to your research questions or methodology.

## Section-Level Word Count Guidelines

| Section | Approximate Length |
|---|---|
| Introduction | 200–400 words |
| Each major theme | 800–1,500 words |
| Synthesis / summary | 400–600 words |
| Transition to Chapter 3 | 100–200 words |
| **Total Chapter 2** | **4,000–8,000 words** (varies by program) |

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Summarizing each article one by one | Organize around themes, not sources |
| "Smith (2018) said X. Jones (2020) said Y." | Synthesize: "Research consistently shows X (Smith, 2018; Jones, 2020)" |
| Including sources tangentially related to your topic | Every source should connect to your constructs or gap |
| No critique of existing literature | Identify weaknesses, contradictions, and limitations in the literature |
| Review ends without connecting to the gap | Always close by articulating what the literature leaves open |
