# Methodology Selector & Justifier

## Overview

Methodology is not a technical choice — it is a philosophical one. The method you choose must be justified by the nature of your research problem, the questions you're asking, and your epistemological stance (your beliefs about how knowledge is produced). This skill walks you through the selection logic, maps your RQs to appropriate designs, and helps you draft the methodology justification for your proposal or Chapter 3.

---

## Intake Questions

1. **What is your central research question?**
2. **Are you trying to explain, predict, describe, explore, or understand?**
3. **Do you have a hypothesis you want to test, or are you generating theory?**
4. **What is your philosophical worldview?** (See framework below — or let Claude infer it from your RQ)
5. **What is your population and what access do you have?**
6. **What resources and timeline do you have for data collection?**
7. **What does your program or institution typically expect?**

## Philosophical Worldview → Methodology Map

| Worldview | Core Belief | Typical Methodology |
|---|---|---|
| **Postpositivism** | Reality exists and can be measured approximately; objectivity is the goal | Quantitative: surveys, experiments, quasi-experiments |
| **Constructivism** | Reality is socially constructed; meaning is co-created | Qualitative: phenomenology, grounded theory, narrative |
| **Transformative** | Research should challenge power and produce social change | Qualitative or mixed; participatory action research |
| **Pragmatism** | What works matters most; use whatever answers the question | Mixed methods |

## Methodology Decision Tree

### Is your RQ exploratory or confirmatory?

**Exploratory** → Qualitative (you don't know what you'll find)
**Confirmatory** → Quantitative (you're testing a hypothesis or relationship)
**Both** → Mixed Methods

---

### Qualitative Design Selection

| Design | Use When | Central Question Form | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phenomenology** | You want to understand the *lived experience* of a phenomenon | "What is the lived experience of X among Y?" | Essence, themes |
| **Grounded Theory** | You want to build a *theory or process model* from the data | "What is the process by which X occurs?" | Substantive theory |
| **Case Study** | You want to understand a *specific case* in depth, in context | "How/why does X occur in this bounded system?" | Rich description, themes |
| **Narrative Inquiry** | You want to understand *individual stories* and how people make meaning | "How do individuals story their experience of X?" | Life narrative, themes |
| **Ethnography** | You want to understand a *culture or group* from the inside | "What are the shared beliefs/practices of this group regarding X?" | Cultural description |
| **Generic Qualitative** | No single design fits; you want description of a phenomenon | "What are the experiences/perceptions of Y regarding X?" | Themes/categories |

---

### Quantitative Design Selection

| Design | Use When | Central Question Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Survey/Correlational** | You want to describe or relate variables in a population | "To what extent is there a relationship between X and Y?" |
| **Causal-Comparative (ex post facto)** | You want to compare groups on a variable; no random assignment | "Is there a difference in Y between Group A and Group B?" |
| **Quasi-Experimental** | You want to test a treatment/intervention without random assignment | "Does X intervention affect Y outcomes?" |
| **True Experiment (RCT)** | You want to establish causality with random assignment | "Does X cause Y under controlled conditions?" |
| **Longitudinal** | You want to track change over time | "How does Y change over time in response to X?" |

---

### Mixed Methods Design Selection

| Design | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **Convergent Parallel** | Collect QUAN and QUAL simultaneously; compare results at interpretation |
| **Explanatory Sequential** | QUAN first to identify patterns; QUAL second to explain them |
| **Exploratory Sequential** | QUAL first to generate constructs; QUAN second to test them at scale |
| **Embedded** | One strand is nested within the other (e.g., QUAL embedded in an RCT) |

## Methodology Justification Template

Use this structure to write the methodology justification in your proposal or Chapter 3:

```
A [qualitative / quantitative / mixed methods] [design name] design was selected
for this study. [Design name] is appropriate when [the purpose this design serves],
which aligns with the purpose of this study to [restate your purpose statement].

[Cite the methodologist who defined/described this design — e.g., Creswell & Poth, 2018;
Moustakas, 1994; Yin, 2018; Creswell & Creswell, 2018.]

An alternative approach — [alternative design] — was considered but rejected because
[specific reason this design would not answer your RQ or fit your context].

This design aligns with a [constructivist / postpositivist / pragmatist] worldview,
which holds that [brief statement of the philosophical stance]. This stance is
appropriate given that [connection to your research problem and questions].
```

|---|---|---|

| Philosophical worldviews | Creswell & Creswell | *Research Design* (2018) Ch. 1–2 |
