# Source Synthesis

## Overview

Synthesis is the intellectual core of doctoral work. It means identifying what a group of sources say collectively — where they agree, where they diverge, what they leave unresolved, and how they build on each other. This skill takes a set of sources (or their abstracts/summaries) and produces a synthesis matrix, thematic clusters, and a narrative outline you can use directly in your literature review.

---

## How to Provide Sources

Paste any of the following:
- Full abstracts
- Your own reading notes
- Key quotes or excerpts
- APA citations with brief summaries
- A list of titles and authors (if Claude has knowledge of them)

Indicate: **How many sources are you working with?** and **What is the central topic or construct you're synthesizing around?**

---

## Synthesis Process

### Step 1: Surface-Level Extraction
For each source, extract:
- **Claim or finding** — what does this source argue or demonstrate?
- **Method** — how was it studied? (qualitative, quantitative, conceptual)
- **Population/context** — who was studied? In what setting?
- **Key constructs** — what variables or concepts are central?
- **Limitations noted** — what do the authors themselves flag as gaps?

### Step 2: Cross-Source Pattern Analysis
After extraction, look across all sources for:

**Convergence points** — where do multiple sources agree?
> "Three studies found a positive relationship between X and Y (Smith, 2018; Jones, 2020; Lee, 2022)."

**Divergence or contradiction** — where do sources disagree?
> "While Smith (2018) found X, Jones (2020) reported no significant relationship, suggesting that organizational size may moderate this effect."

**Methodological patterns** — what methods dominate? What's missing?
> "The majority of studies used cross-sectional survey designs, limiting causal claims."

**Population/context patterns** — who has been studied? Who hasn't?
> "All five studies used corporate, for-profit samples. No study included nonprofit or educational settings."

**Temporal arc** — how has thinking on this topic evolved?
> "Early work conceptualized X as unidimensional (Greenleaf, 1977); later scholars proposed multidimensional models (Liden et al., 2008; van Dierendonck, 2011)."

**Unresolved tensions** — what debates remain open?
> "Scholars disagree about whether X is a trait, a behavior, or a relational construct."

### Step 3: Thematic Clustering
Group sources into 3–5 thematic clusters that could become sections of your literature review. For each cluster:
- Name the theme
- List the sources that belong to it
- Write a 1–2 sentence synthesis claim for the cluster

### Step 4: Gap Identification
Based on the pattern analysis, articulate the gap this literature leaves. Use the `research-gap` taxonomy (population, context, method, temporal, theoretical, variable).

## Synthesis Narrative Structure

After completing the matrix, produce a narrative using this structure:

```
**What is established:**
[2–3 sentences summarizing convergent findings across sources]

**Where scholars disagree:**
[1–2 sentences on contradictions or debates]

**Methodological patterns and limitations:**
[1–2 sentences on dominant methods and what they can/can't answer]

**Who / what has been studied:**
[1 sentence on populations and contexts represented]

**What the literature leaves open:**
[1–2 sentences articulating the gap — connect to your study]

**Thematic Clusters:**
- Cluster 1: [Name] — Sources: [list] — Claim: [sentence]
- Cluster 2: [Name] — Sources: [list] — Claim: [sentence]
- Cluster 3: [Name] — Sources: [list] — Claim: [sentence]
```

---

## Language for Synthesis Writing

Use these constructions to avoid source-by-source summarizing:

- "A body of research supports the claim that…"
- "Scholars have consistently found…"
- "The literature is divided on whether…"
- "A notable limitation across this body of work is…"
- "While [Author A] argues X, [Author B] contends Y, suggesting that…"
- "The preponderance of evidence suggests…"
- "Taken together, these studies indicate…"
- "A gap in this literature is the absence of…"
