# Energy Management

## Purpose
Help the learner identify their peak cognitive hours and align dissertation work with those windows — protecting deep thinking for the work that requires it most.

## The Core Principle
Not all hours are equal. Writing a dissertation chapter requires peak cognitive energy. Reviewing citations, formatting references, and reading emails require much less. Putting deep work in your low-energy hours is one of the most common and costly doctoral mistakes.

**Key insight from Kleitman (1963) and Pink (2018):** Most humans have a predictable performance curve:
- **Peak** — high alertness, best analytical and creative thinking
- **Trough** — mid-day energy dip, low mood and focus
- **Recovery** — second wind, often creative but not analytical

## Energy Chronotypes

Ask the learner: "Are you a morning person, a night person, or somewhere in between?"

| Chronotype | Peak | Trough | Recovery |
|-----------|------|--------|---------|
| **Morning lark** | 6–10 AM | 1–3 PM | 4–6 PM |
| **Third bird (average)** | 9 AM–12 PM | 2–4 PM | 5–7 PM |
| **Night owl** | 3–9 PM | 10 AM–12 PM | 10 PM–12 AM |

*Note: Chronotype is biological, not a matter of discipline. Working against it yields lower quality output and faster depletion.*

## Task-Energy Alignment

### Peak Hours → Deepest Work
- Original writing (new sections, new arguments)
- Data analysis (coding, statistical analysis, interpretation)
- Synthesizing complex literature
- Developing theoretical arguments
- Proposal or chapter drafts

### Recovery Hours → Creative or Engaging Work
- Reading new sources
- Concept mapping and outlining
- Revising and editing (not as deep as drafting)
- Working on sections you enjoy
- Brainstorming and generative thinking

### Trough Hours → Shallow Work Only
- Formatting references
- Managing citations in Zotero/Mendeley
- Sending emails to committee
- Reviewing and organizing notes
- Administrative doctoral tasks

## Process

### Step 1 — Audit Current Patterns
Ask:
1. "What time of day do you feel sharpest?"
2. "What time do you typically try to write now?"
3. "When do you feel most drained?"
4. "What are you doing during your peak hours right now?"

Often, learners discover they are spending their peak hours in meetings, responding to work email, or handling family responsibilities — and trying to write in the trough.

### Step 2 — Map the Energy Week

Build a weekly energy map:
| Day | Peak Window | Trough Window | Recovery Window | Currently Used For |
|-----|------------|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Monday | 8–11 AM | 1–3 PM | 4–6 PM | |
| Tuesday | ... | | | |

Identify which windows are *actually available* for dissertation work — not ideal, but real.

### Step 3 — Protect Peak Time

**Scheduling rules for peak time:**
- No meetings during peak hours if avoidable
- No email until peak work is complete
- First task of the peak session is always the hardest, most important thing

**Protecting strategies:**
- Block the calendar as "Deep Work — Do Not Schedule"
- Communicate the block to family/colleagues
- Don't check phone or email before writing is complete

### Step 4 — Design the Optimal Doctoral Week

Build a sample week based on:
- Identified peak windows
- Available days/times
- Deep work vs. shallow work allocation
- Recovery and rest (non-negotiable for sustained output)

**Sample optimized week (morning lark, part-time student):**
| Time Block | Activity |
|-----------|---------|
| Mon–Fri 6–7:30 AM | Deep writing (peak hours protected) |
| Mon–Fri 8–5 PM | Work / other responsibilities |
| Tue/Thu 7–8 PM | Reading and source processing (recovery) |
| Saturday 9 AM–12 PM | Longer writing session (3-pomodoro block) |
| Sunday | Rest — complete dissertation off (recovery for the week) |

### Step 5 — Protect Recovery
Doctoral learners chronically undervalue rest. Cognitive depletion is real.

**Non-negotiable recovery practices:**
- 7–8 hours of sleep (consolidates memory and learning)
- At least one dissertation-free day per week
- Physical movement (exercise improves cognitive performance)
- Deliberate transitions between work and personal life

## Output Format
1. **Chronotype identification** (lark / third bird / owl)
2. **Energy map** (peak / trough / recovery by day)
3. **Current misalignment** (where peak hours are being wasted)
4. **Realigned weekly schedule** (deep work in peak hours)
5. **Peak time protection strategies** (2–3 specific tactics)
6. **Recovery commitments** (what will be protected)
