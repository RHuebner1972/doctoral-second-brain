# Literature Review

## Purpose
Guide the learner through planning, organizing, and drafting a scholarly literature review that synthesizes — not summarizes — the existing knowledge base.

## The Cardinal Rule
A literature review is NOT an annotated bibliography. It does NOT describe what each source said. It **synthesizes** sources around themes, tensions, agreements, and gaps to build the conceptual foundation for the study.

> Wrong: "Smith (2020) found that... Jones (2019) argued that... Brown (2021) showed that..."
> Right: "Scholars consistently identify [theme] as central to [phenomenon] (Smith, 2020; Jones, 2019; Brown, 2021), though disagreement persists regarding [tension]..."

## Structure Options

### Option A — Thematic (most common)
Organize by concept or theme, regardless of chronology. Best for topics with rich, cross-cutting literature.
```
Introduction
├── Theme 1: [e.g., Servant Leadership Behaviors]
├── Theme 2: [e.g., Organizational Outcomes]
├── Theme 3: [e.g., Contextual Moderators]
├── Theoretical Framework
└── Summary and Gap
```

### Option B — Chronological
Organize by historical progression. Best for tracing evolution of a concept or theory.

### Option C — Methodological
Organize by research design. Best when the gap is methodological.

### Option D — Conceptual (Model-Driven)
Organize around a theoretical model. Best when applying a specific framework to a new context.

## Process

### Step 1 — Inventory Sources
Ask the learner:
1. How many sources do you have? What are the key ones?
2. What themes keep appearing across sources?
3. Where do authors disagree?
4. What theoretical frameworks have been applied?

### Step 2 — Build a Synthesis Matrix
Create a table:
| Source | Theme 1 | Theme 2 | Theme 3 | Method | Key Finding | Gap Noted |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|--------|-------------|-----------|

### Step 3 — Create an Outline
- Map sources to themes
- Identify which themes have strong coverage vs. thin coverage
- Identify where the gap lives in the structure

### Step 4 — Draft Section by Section

**Introduction paragraph:**
> State the purpose of the review, the scope (what's included/excluded), and the organization.

**Theme sections:**
> Open with a topic sentence that names the theme and its significance.
> Build an argument using synthesized evidence from multiple sources.
> Close with a transition that connects to the next theme or builds toward the gap.

**Gap and summary paragraph:**
> Draw the thread from the reviewed literature to the identified gap.
> Make explicit what is NOT known and why this study is needed.

### Step 5 — Citation and APA Check
- Every claim needs a citation
- Direct quotes need page numbers
- Paraphrases do not need page numbers but should
- Use author-date format throughout

## Quality Criteria
- [ ] Every paragraph has a clear argument (not just description)
- [ ] At least 3 sources per major claim
- [ ] Tension and disagreement in the literature are named
- [ ] Theoretical framework section is present and connected
- [ ] Gap is explicitly stated and traceable to the review

## Output Format
1. **Recommended structure** (outline with themes named)
2. **Synthesis matrix** (filled with learner's sources)
3. **Draft introduction** (first paragraph of the lit review)
4. **Draft of one theme section** (as a model)
5. **Gap-to-study bridge paragraph**
6. **Next steps** — missing sources, sections to expand
