# Note to Insight

## Purpose
Transform raw reading notes — bullet points, highlights, half-thoughts — into structured, retrievable insight artifacts that connect to the dissertation and accumulate into a usable second brain.

## The Problem With Raw Notes
Reading notes in their raw form are:
- Locked inside the source they came from
- Disconnected from the dissertation argument
- Structured as summaries of what the author said, not insights about what it means
- Impossible to synthesize later

Processed notes are:
- Organized around *ideas*, not sources
- Connected to research questions and themes
- Written in a voice ready to draft from
- Tagged and retrievable

## The Three Levels of Notes

**Level 1 — Literature Notes (capture):**
What did the source say? Close paraphrase, key quotes, core arguments.
*Written in the author's frame.*

**Level 2 — Permanent Notes (insight):**
What does this mean for my research? What do I now think?
*Written in the learner's own frame.*

**Level 3 — Synthesis Notes (connection):**
How does this connect to other sources, to the gap, to the research questions?
*Written as a multi-source argument.*

## Process

### Step 1 — Receive the Raw Notes
Ask the learner to paste or describe their raw notes from a reading session.

### Step 2 — Extract the Core Claim
From the raw notes, identify:
- What is the author's central argument or finding? (1 sentence)
- What evidence or method supports it?
- What are the stated limitations?
- What is the most quotable passage?

### Step 3 — Write a Literature Note
Format:
```
SOURCE: Author (year) — Title (shortened)
CLAIM: [One sentence — what the author argues or found]
EVIDENCE: [Brief — how they established this]
METHOD: [Design and sample, briefly]
LIMITATION: [What the authors acknowledge as a weakness]
KEY QUOTE: "[exact quote]" (p. X)
RELEVANCE: [How this connects to my research questions or topic — in my own words]
```

### Step 4 — Write the Permanent Note (Insight)
This is the most important step. Ask:
- "What do I now think about [concept] having read this?"
- "Does this confirm, challenge, or complicate what I've read elsewhere?"
- "What would I write if I were trying to explain this idea to a colleague who hasn't read this?"

Write 2–5 sentences in your own words. No jargon unless you mean it.

> Example: "Bandura's concept of self-efficacy is not just confidence — it's domain-specific. Someone can have high self-efficacy for writing but low self-efficacy for defending in front of a committee. This matters for my study because participants' engagement with professional development may be driven less by global motivation than by their specific confidence in the developmental area being addressed."

### Step 5 — Create the Connection Note
Ask:
- "Where does this connect to other sources I've read?"
- "Which of my themes or research questions does this inform?"
- "Does this reinforce a claim I'm building or introduce tension?"

Write a brief connection note:
```
CONNECTS TO: [other source / theme / RQ]
RELATIONSHIP: [confirms / challenges / extends / qualifies]
IMPLICATION: [What this means for my argument]
```

### Step 6 — Tag and File
Assign:
- **Theme tags:** (e.g., #servant-leadership, #faculty-retention, #qualitative-methodology)
- **Dissertation section:** (Chapter 2 / Theme 2 / Theoretical Framework)
- **Status:** (new / in-progress / ready-to-cite)

## Output Format
1. **Literature note** (structured, source-oriented)
2. **Permanent note** (insight, in the learner's voice)
3. **Connection note** (links to other sources and RQs)
4. **Tags and filing recommendation**
5. **Draft sentence** — one sentence that could go directly into the dissertation, attributed to this source
